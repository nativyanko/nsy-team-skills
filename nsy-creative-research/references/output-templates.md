# Output Document Templates

This is the most important reference file. It defines the exact structure, minimum depth, and quality standard for every output document the research skill produces. When in doubt about whether a document is "deep enough", refer back to these templates.

**The quality test for every document:** If a world-class direct response copywriter opened this document with zero other context, would they have everything they need to write a winning ad? If no, the document isn't deep enough.

---

## Document 1: [Client Name] - Brand Overview and Product Deep Dive

### Purpose
Everything about the product, brand, offer structure, and positioning. This is the foundation document that ensures the Claude project understands exactly what it's selling.

### Required Sections

**1.1 Brand Summary**
- Brand name, tagline, and one-paragraph positioning statement
- Founded when, by whom (if relevant to brand story)
- Brand personality/voice in 2-3 sentences
- Country/market focus

**1.2 Product Details**
For each product (if multiple), provide:
- Product name
- What it is (physical description, format - serum, capsule, powder, etc.)
- What it does (primary benefit in one sentence)
- Key ingredients or components (list with one-line description of each)
- How to use it (application/dosage instructions)
- What makes it different from alternatives (the USP or unique mechanism, if known at this stage)

**1.3 Offer Structure**
- Hero product price
- Bundle options and pricing (e.g., "Buy 2 Get 1 Free at $XX/bottle")
- Subscription options and pricing
- Discounts and promotions currently running
- Free shipping threshold
- Money-back guarantee details (duration, conditions)
- Bonuses included with purchase (if any)
- Upsells and cross-sells

**1.4 Brand Claims**
List every claim the brand makes on their website, landing pages, and ads:
- Headline claims (the big promise)
- Supporting claims
- Testimonial claims (what customers say on the site)
- Scientific/clinical claims (if any)
- Authority claims (endorsed by, featured in, etc.)
- Note which claims have evidence behind them and which are unsubstantiated

**1.5 Website and Landing Page Analysis**
- Homepage: what's the first thing a visitor sees? What's the main CTA?
- Product page: how is the product presented? What's above the fold?
- Advertorial/VSL: does the brand use long-form sales pages? What approach?
- Trust signals: what social proof, badges, certifications appear?
- Brand voice observations: formal or casual? Technical or conversational? How do they address the customer?

**1.6 Competitive Positioning**
- Where this brand sits relative to competitors (premium, mid-range, budget)
- What they emphasize that competitors don't
- What they're missing that competitors have

### Minimum Depth
- At least 2 pages of content
- Every section filled with specific details, not generic descriptions
- All pricing captured with exact numbers
- At least 10 specific brand claims listed
- Website analysis based on actual page content, not assumptions

## Document 2: [Client Name] - Target Audience and Customer Psychographics

### Purpose
Deep psychological profile of the target customer. This document should make the reader feel like they KNOW this person - their fears, hopes, language, beliefs, and objections. Everything here is backed by evidence from research.

### Required Sections

**2.1 Customer Avatars**
Create 2-3 named customer avatars. Each avatar includes:
- Name, age, life situation (e.g., "Sarah, 42, working mother of two in suburban Ohio")
- A 3-4 sentence backstory that captures their relationship with the problem
- Their primary pain point
- Their primary desire
- What would make them buy today
- What would make them scroll past the ad

**2.2 Demographic Profile**
- Age range
- Gender split
- Income level
- Education level
- Life stage (single, married, parents, empty nesters, etc.)
- Geographic concentration
- Occupation patterns

**2.3 Psychographic Profile (RMBC Framework)**

**Attitudes (Religious, Political, Social, Economic)**
How these attitudes affect what messaging resonates. What values to lean into, what to avoid.

**Hopes and Dreams**
Specific, vivid, emotionally charged. What does their ideal future look like where this problem is solved? Include at least 5 real quotes from forums/social media showing how they describe their desired outcome.

**Victories and Failures**
What they've tried before. Small wins they've had. Crushing failures. Include at least 3 real quotes showing their frustration with past attempts.

**Outside Forces They Blame**
External villains they believe are responsible for their problem. Include real quotes showing this belief pattern.

**Prejudices (Biases)**
What types of people, brands, or approaches they instinctively distrust. What triggers immediate skepticism.

**Core Beliefs**
1-3 sentences summing up their fundamental worldview as it relates to this problem.

**2.4 Existing Solutions They've Tried**
For each solution:
- What it is
- Why they tried it
- What they liked about it
- Why it wasn't enough
- Real customer quotes about it

**2.5 Horror Stories**
Real stories of things going wrong with existing solutions. At least 3 stories with real quotes or detailed descriptions. These become ad hook material.

**2.6 The Efficacy Paradox**
Does this market keep buying solutions they don't fully believe in? If so, explain the paradox and what it means for positioning. What would make them believe THIS product is the credible exception?

**2.7 Curiosity Angles**
At least 3 curiosity angles discovered during research:
- The story/angle
- Why it's compelling for this market
- How it could be used in ad creative (hook idea)

**2.8 Corruption Angles**
At least 2 corruption angles discovered:
- The external force or villain
- Evidence that the market already believes this narrative
- How it could be used in ad creative

**2.9 Primary Promises**
At least 5 specific promises written in the customer's language:
- The promise
- The pain point it addresses
- The desired future state it unlocks

**2.10 Objections and Rebuttals**
Every major objection the market will have, paired with a rebuttal strategy:
- "It's too expensive" -> [rebuttal]
- "I've tried things like this before and they don't work" -> [rebuttal]
- "How do I know this isn't another scam?" -> [rebuttal]
- [Additional objections specific to this product/market]

**2.11 Language Guide**
- **Words and phrases TO USE** (language that resonates, validates, and connects)
- **Words and phrases TO AVOID** (language that feels patronizing, clinical, or tone-deaf)
- **Emotional register** - how much emotion is appropriate? Should copy be empathetic, empowering, urgent, scientific, conversational?

### Minimum Depth
- At least 4-5 pages of content
- 2-3 named avatars with backstories
- All 16 RMBC questions answered with evidence
- At least 20 real customer quotes embedded throughout
- At least 3 curiosity angles with story details
- At least 2 corruption angles with evidence
- At least 5 primary promises in customer language
- At least 5 objections with rebuttals

## Document 3: [Client Name] - Customer Voice (Reviews)

### Purpose
A organized collection of real customer language from reviews, structured by theme. This document gives the Claude project direct access to how customers actually talk about this product and category.

### Required Sections

**3.1 Review Sources**
List where reviews were gathered from (Trustpilot, Amazon, brand website, Reddit, etc.) and approximate volume.

**3.2 Overall Sentiment Summary**
Quick snapshot: What percentage positive vs negative? What's the dominant theme?

**3.3 Positive Themes**
For each positive theme identified (aim for at least 5 themes):
- **Theme name** (e.g., "Fast visible results", "Texture and feel", "Value for money")
- **Summary** - 1-2 sentences describing the pattern
- **Quotes** - at least 5 real customer quotes that represent this theme (preserved exactly as written, with source noted)

**3.4 Negative Themes**
For each negative theme identified (aim for at least 3 themes):
- **Theme name** (e.g., "Slow shipping", "Didn't work for me", "Too expensive")
- **Summary** - 1-2 sentences describing the pattern
- **Quotes** - at least 3 real customer quotes (preserved exactly as written)
- **Objection implication** - what this means for ad creative (what to preempt or address)

**3.5 Recurring Language Patterns**
- Words and phrases that appear repeatedly across positive reviews
- Words and phrases that appear repeatedly across negative reviews
- How customers describe the PROBLEM in their own words
- How customers describe the RESULT in their own words

**3.6 Strongest Testimonials**
The 5-10 most compelling customer quotes that could be used directly in ad creative. For each:
- The full quote
- Why it's strong (specific result, emotional language, relatable situation)
- Suggested use (hook, social proof section, testimonial ad, etc.)

### Minimum Depth
- At least 5 positive themes with 5+ quotes each
- At least 3 negative themes with 3+ quotes each
- At least 30 total real customer quotes
- Recurring language patterns documented
- 5-10 strongest testimonials highlighted with usage suggestions

## Document 4: [Client Name] - Customer Voice (Ad Comments)

*Only produced when strategist provides ad comment export CSV.*

### Purpose
Mine ad comments for objections, questions, social proof, and language patterns. Ad comments reveal what people think when they first encounter the brand's marketing.

### Required Sections

**4.1 Data Source**
How many ads, time period, total number of comments analyzed.

**4.2 Most Common Questions**
Questions people ask in comments, ranked by frequency. Each question is an objection the next ad should preemptively answer.

**4.3 Positive Social Proof Comments**
Comments from people who've tried the product and are praising it publicly. These are powerful because they come from real buyers in an ad context.

**4.4 Negative Comments and Objections**
Complaints, skepticism, and pushback. Organized by theme. Each one is an objection to address in future creative.

**4.5 Tag-a-Friend Patterns**
Who are people tagging and why? This reveals who the buyer is buying for (themselves vs. gift for partner/parent).

**4.6 Language Patterns**
How do commenters describe the product, problem, or promise in their own words?

### Minimum Depth
- At least 5 common questions catalogued
- At least 10 positive social proof comments
- At least 10 negative comments organized by theme
- Language patterns documented

## Document 5: [Client Name] - Customer Voice (Post-Purchase Surveys)

*Only produced when strategist provides survey data.*

### Purpose
Understand what convinced buyers to purchase, what almost stopped them, and how they describe the product after using it.

### Required Sections

**5.1 Data Source**
Survey type, number of responses, time period.

**5.2 What Convinced Them to Buy**
The tipping point. What was the final thing that made them click "purchase"? Organized by frequency.

**5.3 What Almost Stopped Them**
The biggest objection they had to overcome. This is the most valuable section - these are the objections that matter most because they came from people who actually bought.

**5.4 Most Valued Benefit**
After using the product, what benefit do they value most? (This often differs from what the brand emphasizes.)

**5.5 How They Describe the Product**
In their own words, not the brand's words. What would they tell a friend?

**5.6 Key Quotes**
The most insightful and usable quotes from survey responses.

### Minimum Depth
- All sections filled with specific data points
- At least 10 key quotes preserved

## Document 6: [Client Name] - Competitive Landscape

### Purpose
Comprehensive competitive intelligence. Not just a list of names - deep analysis of messaging, positioning, pricing, and language patterns across the competitive set.

### Required Sections

**6.1 Competitor Profiles**
For EACH competitor (minimum 5), provide a complete profile:

**[Competitor Name]**
- Website URL
- One-line positioning statement
- Target demographic (age, gender, psychographic)
- Primary acquisition funnels (Meta, Google, influencer, organic, etc.)
- Core promise/headline claim
- Recurring hooks and angles (at least 3 specific hooks they use repeatedly)
- Pricing: hero product price, bundles, subscriptions, discounts
- Guarantee terms
- Customer love (at least 3 real review quotes showing what people praise)
- Customer hate (at least 3 real review quotes showing complaints)
- Specific repeated language: exact phrases and claims they use across multiple assets
- Estimated revenue (if findable)

**6.2 Competitive Positioning Map**
Where each competitor sits on key dimensions:
- Price point (budget to premium)
- Positioning (clinical/scientific vs. natural/lifestyle)
- Messaging tone (fear-based vs. aspiration-based)
- Primary differentiator

**6.3 Common Industry Language**
Phrases and claims that EVERY competitor uses. These are table stakes - the brand must match them to be taken seriously.

**6.4 Differentiable Language**
Phrases and claims that only 1-2 competitors use. These could be ownable angles.

**6.5 Messaging Gaps**
What is NO competitor saying that the market wants to hear? These are the biggest opportunities.

**6.6 Creative Format Patterns**
What ad formats dominate in this competitive set? (UGC, talking head, before/after, lifestyle, clinical demos, infographic-style)

**6.7 Opportunities for This Brand**
Based on all the above: what angles, claims, or positions could this brand own? What can they say that nobody else is saying?

### Minimum Depth
- At least 5 competitor profiles with all data points filled
- At least 3 customer quotes per competitor
- At least 3 recurring hooks per competitor
- Positioning map completed
- Gaps and opportunities identified with specific recommendations

## Document 7: [Client Name] - Market Awareness and Sophistication

### Purpose
Where the market sits on the Schwartz awareness spectrum and what this means for creative strategy.

### Required Sections

**7.1 Awareness Stage Breakdown**
Each stage with:
- Estimated percentage of TAM
- Reasoning (what signals support this estimate)
- Example of a person at this stage for this specific product

**7.2 Final Assessment**
Which awareness stage does the MAJORITY of the reachable Meta audience fall into?

**7.3 Creative Strategy Implications**
Based on the awareness assessment:
- Recommended ad length (short, medium, long)
- What to lead with (problem, mechanism, product, or offer)
- How much education is needed
- Funnel complexity recommended
- What hooks will work at this awareness level
- What won't work (e.g., "Don't lead with the product name if the market is only problem-aware")

**7.4 Platform-Specific Recommendations**
- Meta cold traffic: what awareness level to target and how
- Retargeting: what awareness level these people are at
- If applicable: Google, TikTok considerations

**7.5 Evidence and Signals**
The specific data points supporting the assessment: search trends, social media volume, forum activity, influencer content volume, category growth data.

### Minimum Depth
- All 5 awareness stages described with market-specific examples
- Percentage estimates with reasoning
- Clear creative strategy implications (not vague suggestions)
- At least 3 specific evidence signals cited

## Document 8: [Client Name] - Ad Account Insights and Proven Angles

*Only produced when strategist provides ad account performance data.*

### Purpose
Analyze what's already working (and not working) in the brand's ad account to inform new creative direction.

### Required Sections

**8.1 Data Overview**
Time period, total spend, number of ads analyzed, overall ROAS.

**8.2 Top Performing Ads**
For each top performer:
- Ad name/identifier
- Hook/angle used
- Format (UGC, static, lifestyle, etc.)
- Key metrics (spend, ROAS, CTR, CPA)
- Why it likely works (hypothesis)

**8.3 Worst Performing Ads**
For each underperformer:
- What angle/approach was tried
- Why it likely failed (hypothesis)

**8.4 Winning Angles**
Patterns across top performers: what themes, hooks, or approaches consistently work?

**8.5 Fatigued Creative**
Ads that used to work but have declined. What needs refreshing?

**8.6 Missing Angles**
Based on the psychographic and competitive research: what angles have NEVER been tested that should be?

**8.7 Format Gaps**
What ad formats haven't been tried? What formats consistently outperform?

### Minimum Depth
- All top and bottom performers analyzed with hypotheses
- Pattern analysis completed
- At least 3 missing angles recommended
- Format gaps identified

## Document 9: [Client Name] - Science, Mechanism and Objections

*Only produced for health/wellness/supplement brands.*

### Purpose
Evidence-backed claims for each ingredient, organized for easy use in ad copy. Plus science-backed objection rebuttals.

### Required Sections

**9.1 Ingredient Claims**
For EACH ingredient:

**[Ingredient Name]**
- What it is (one sentence)
- **Claim 1:** [Digestible claim statement]
  - Study: [Author, year, journal]
  - Key finding: [Specific result, e.g., "32% reduction in wrinkle depth after 8 weeks"]
  - Study type: [RCT / Clinical trial / In-vitro / Animal study]
- **Claim 2:** [Next claim]
  - [Same format]
- [Continue for all claims]

**9.2 Mechanism Support**
How do the ingredients work together to support the product's overall approach/mechanism? Connect the dots between individual ingredients and the bigger story.

**9.3 Science-Backed Objection Rebuttals**
Common objections with scientific evidence to counter them:
- "Natural ingredients can't really work as well as [medical treatment]" -> [Evidence]
- "There's no proof this actually works" -> [Evidence]
- [Product-specific objections]

**9.4 Compliance Notes**
Claims that are well-supported vs. claims that are a stretch. Flag anything that could get an ad disapproved on Meta.

### Minimum Depth
- Every ingredient covered
- At least 2 studies per ingredient from NCBI/peer-reviewed sources
- Key statistics highlighted (percentages, timeframes, sample sizes)
- Claims written in ad-ready language (not academic jargon)
- Compliance flags noted

- Health claim restrictions

## Document 11: [Client Name] - Unified Research Document

### Purpose
THE most important output. This single document synthesizes all research into one strategic brief that powers all downstream copy generation. This is what a copywriter or AI reads to understand everything they need to write winning ads.

### Required Sections

**11.1 Target Market Demographic Overview**
Focused on the awareness segments most relevant for Meta ads (as determined by the Market Awareness research). Demographics + psychographic snapshot. Written as a profile, not a data table.

**11.2 Psychographic Overview**

**Pain Points (Dimensionalized)**
Not a bullet list of generic problems. Each pain point should be a vivid, emotional description with real customer quotes embedded. Show how the pain point affects their daily life, relationships, self-image, and future outlook.

Example depth (from a different niche):
> "Feel embarrassed, ashamed, and humiliated about their weight, especially when comparing themselves to thinner women. They avoid mirrors, dread photos, and have stopped accepting invitations to pool parties or beach vacations. As one woman on Reddit put it: 'I haven't worn a swimsuit in 6 years. My daughter asks me to come swimming and I make excuses every time. She's going to remember that.'"

NOT this:
> "They feel self-conscious about their weight."

**Hopes and Dreams**
What their ideal future looks like where the problem is solved. How they would look and feel. What else would be true about their life. Include real quotes showing how they describe this future state.

**Self-Perception and Identity**
How they see themselves. What validates them. What feels patronizing.

**Language to Use**
Specific words, phrases, and tones that resonate.

**Language to Avoid**
Specific words, phrases, and tones that trigger rejection.

**Primary Promises**
The specific outcomes they want to hear, written in their language. At least 5.

**Primary Objections**
Every major objection with a rebuttal strategy. At least 5.

**11.3 Existing Solutions and Why They're Inadequate**
What the market has tried, what was good about it, and specifically why it fell short. This section sets up the "everything else has failed, but this is different because..." positioning.

**11.4 Curiosity and Corruption Angles**
The strongest angles from research with enough detail that a copywriter could build a hook, lead, or entire ad concept from each one. At least 3 curiosity angles and 2 corruption angles.

### What to EXCLUDE
- TAM size numbers
- Marketing strategy recommendations
- General market background or industry overview
- Academic research summaries that don't translate to copy
- Anything a copywriter wouldn't use to write a better ad

### Minimum Depth
- At least 5-7 pages of dense, specific content
- Every pain point dimensionalized with real quotes (not one-line bullets)
- At least 15 real customer quotes embedded throughout
- At least 5 primary promises in customer language
- At least 5 objections with rebuttals
- At least 3 curiosity angles with full story details
- At least 2 corruption angles with evidence
- Language guide completed

## Document 12: [Client Name] - Project Instructions

### Purpose
The meta-document that configures the Claude project. When uploaded as the first file, it tells the Claude project how to behave, what rules to follow, and how to use all the other documents.

### Required Sections

**12.1 Project Identity**
"You are the creative strategist for [Client Name]. Your job is to produce high-converting direct response ad scripts, hooks, and creative concepts for Meta ads."

**12.2 Document Map**
List every document in the project and what it contains, so the project knows where to look for specific information.

**12.3 Rules**
- Always read the research documents before writing any scripts
- Every script must target one specific customer avatar
- Lead with the problem, not the product (unless targeting product-aware audiences)
- Use the customer's language from the Customer Voice documents
- Reference specific claims from the Science document (for health/wellness brands)
- Never use em dashes
- [Add any client-specific compliance rules discovered during research]
- [Add any tone/voice requirements]
- [Add any format requirements specific to this client]

**12.4 Awareness-Level Guidance**
Based on the Market Awareness research: "The majority of this market is [awareness level]. This means ads should [lead with X], be [length], and include [amount of education]."

**12.5 Winning Patterns**
If ad account data was analyzed: "These angles have proven to work: [list]. These formats perform best: [list]. Avoid: [list of fatigued or underperforming approaches]."

**12.6 Compliance Guardrails**
Any claims to avoid, Meta policy considerations, or client-specific restrictions.

### Minimum Depth
- Every section filled with client-specific details (not generic instructions)
- Document map reflects the actual documents uploaded
- Rules are specific and actionable

## Project Assembly Guide

### Purpose
A comprehensive checklist of everything that should go into the final Claude project. The skill presents this to the strategist at the end and tracks what they've uploaded.

### Research Documents (Produced by This Skill)
Download each and upload to the project knowledge base:

1. Project Instructions (upload FIRST)
2. Unified Research Document
3. Brand Overview and Product Deep Dive
4. Target Audience and Customer Psychographics
5. Customer Voice (Reviews)
6. Customer Voice (Ad Comments) - if produced
7. Customer Voice (Post-Purchase Surveys) - if produced
8. Competitive Landscape
9. Market Awareness and Sophistication
10. Ad Account Insights and Proven Angles - if produced
11. Science, Mechanism and Objections - if produced
12. Brand DNA for Image Gen - if produced

### Raw Data Files (Strategist Adds Directly)
These give the project access to every individual data point, not just summaries:

- [ ] Review CSVs (full dataset from Amazon, Trustpilot, or internal)
- [ ] Ad comment export CSVs (from Meta Ads Manager)
- [ ] Post-purchase survey data (CSV or responses)
- [ ] Call transcripts (onboarding calls, strategy calls, monthly reviews)
- [ ] Onboarding form responses

### Winning Creative (Strategist Adds)
Previous work that shows what's already proven:

- [ ] Top-performing scripts from previous batches
- [ ] Winning ad screenshots or descriptions
- [ ] Any existing creative briefs that worked well

### Skill Files (Strategist Adds)
The tools that let the project produce outputs:

- [ ] Creative Strategy skill file
- [ ] Brief Builder skill file
- [ ] Static Ad Generator skill file

### Custom Instructions
Client-specific project instructions (generated by this skill based on research findings).

### Reminder Protocol
At the end of the research process, the skill should:
1. List everything above as a checklist
2. Specifically remind the strategist about any raw data files they shared during the research conversation: "You shared a [file type] during our research - make sure to add that to the project too."
3. Walk through each item and confirm it's uploaded before signing off
4. Explain WHY the raw data matters: "The research documents are my analysis, but the raw CSVs give the project direct access to every individual customer quote and data point. That means when it writes a script, it can pull from hundreds of real customer voices, not just my summaries."
