# Research Frameworks

This document contains the theoretical frameworks that power every phase of the NSY creative research process. These are not optional reading - they are the lens through which all research should be conducted and all output documents should be structured.

---

## 1. Eugene Schwartz Market Awareness Stages (DTC Adaptation)

The five stages of market awareness, originally from Eugene Schwartz's Breakthrough Advertising, adapted for DTC ecommerce brands. This is the single most important framework for determining creative strategy.

**Critical DTC modification:** In direct-to-consumer marketing, "Product Aware" does NOT mean they know about YOUR specific brand. Even brands doing $20-30M in revenue are unknown to the vast majority of their addressable market. "Product Aware" means they know the CATEGORY of product exists (e.g., they know peptide eye creams exist, but they've never heard of your brand specifically).

### The Five Stages

**1. Unaware (typically 30-50% of TAM)**
The prospect does not recognize they have a problem. They go about their daily routine without noticing or thinking about the issue your product solves. The problem might be present, but it hasn't registered consciously.

- Example (eye serum): A 38-year-old woman goes about her daily routine without noticing the fine lines around her eyes. She might catch glimpses in mirrors but doesn't register any changes.
- Example (supplements): Someone experiences low energy but attributes it to being busy or getting older. They don't connect it to a nutritional gap.
- Creative implication: Extremely difficult to reach via direct response. This is more of a branding/content play. Do not target this segment with Meta ads unless using awareness-stage content.

**2. Problem Aware (typically 40-60% of TAM)**
The prospect knows something is wrong but doesn't know what to do about it. They've noticed the problem and it bothers them, but they haven't researched solutions.

- Example (eye serum): She starts noticing crow's feet in photos. She thinks "When did I start looking so tired?" but hasn't researched what to do about it.
- Example (supplements): They know their gut feels off, they're bloated after meals, but they haven't looked into gut health supplements.
- Creative implication: This is where most Meta cold traffic lives. Ads need to AGITATE the problem first, then introduce the solution. Lead with the pain, not the product. Longer copy needed to move them through the awareness stages.

**3. Solution Aware (typically 60-99% depending on category)**
The prospect knows solutions exist but doesn't know which one is best. They've seen ads, heard from friends, or done basic research. They know the category exists.

- Example (eye serum): She knows eye creams exist and that there are anti-aging treatments. She's seen ads for retinol, hyaluronic acid, procedures. She understands solutions are out there but doesn't know which approach is best.
- Example (supplements): They know probiotics exist, they've heard of gut health, they've seen ads for various supplements. They're aware of the solution category but overwhelmed by choices.
- Creative implication: Ads can lead with the solution/mechanism rather than the problem. Focus on WHY your approach is different from everything else they've tried. This is where unique mechanisms shine.

**4. Product Aware (typically 5-20% depending on category maturity)**
The prospect knows the CATEGORY of product well. They understand the specific type of solution and are comparing options. For very mature categories (like GLP-1 weight loss), this percentage can be much higher.

- Example (eye serum): She's learned about peptides specifically for eye care. She knows peptide-based eye creams are a category and is researching different peptide serums.
- Example (supplements): They know about specific probiotic strains, they understand CFU counts, they're comparing specific gut health products.
- Creative implication: Shorter copy works. Can lead with the product and its differentiators. Static ads and simple landing pages can convert. Less education needed.

**5. Most Aware (typically <1-5%)**
The prospect knows about your specific brand, has researched you, read reviews, and is ready to make a purchase decision. They just need a push (offer, urgency, social proof).

- Creative implication: Retargeting ads. Offer-focused creative. Testimonial ads. Price-drop notifications. These people need a reason to act NOW, not more education.

### Awareness-to-Creative Strategy Matrix

| Awareness Level | Copy Length Needed | Lead With | Ad Format | Funnel Complexity |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Unaware | Not viable for DR | N/A | Content/branding only | N/A |
| Problem Aware | Long (VSL, advertorial) | The PROBLEM and its consequences | Video ads, long-form | Full funnel: ad -> advertorial/VSL -> landing page |
| Solution Aware | Medium-long | The MECHANISM (why your approach is different) | Video ads, medium-form | Ad -> landing page with mechanism explanation |
| Product Aware | Short-medium | The PRODUCT and its differentiators | Static + short video | Ad -> product page or simple landing page |
| Most Aware | Short | The OFFER (price, urgency, social proof) | Static, retargeting | Ad -> checkout page |

### Key Insight: The Copy Journey

Your creative is doing the work of taking someone from their current awareness stage to "Most Aware" (ready to buy). If they start at Problem Aware, you're moving them through 3-4 stages. If they start Product Aware, only 1-2 stages. This directly determines how long your ad needs to be and how much education it needs to include.

### Platform-Awareness Connection

- **Meta cold traffic:** Primarily Problem Aware and Solution Aware. Your ads must do heavy lifting.
- **Google Search:** Self-selecting into Product Aware and Most Aware. Less copy needed.
- **TikTok:** Mix of Problem Aware and Solution Aware, but attention spans are shorter. Need to compress the journey.
- **Retargeting:** Most Aware. Lead with offer and urgency.

## 2. RMBC Psychographic Research Framework

The psychographic deep dive is where you discover the language, beliefs, fears, hopes, and objections of the target market. This framework comes from Stefan Georgi's RMBC Method. Every question must be answered with EVIDENCE - real quotes, real forum posts, real review language. No AI-paraphrased summaries.

### Section A: Insights Into the Demographic

**Q1: Who Is Your Customer?**
Demographic baseline. Age, gender, income level, life stage, education, occupation. But go beyond demographics into psychographics - what kind of person are they? What do they value? How do they spend their time?

**Q2: What Attitudes Do They Have? (Religious, Political, Social, Economic)**
This directly affects messaging tone and references. If the market leans conservative, you can reference certain values positively. If they lean progressive, different values. For health/wellness: do they trust traditional medicine or are they skeptical of big pharma? For biz-opp: do they believe in the American dream or feel the system is rigged?

**Q3: What Are Their Hopes and Dreams?**
Not generic hopes. Specific, vivid, emotionally charged hopes. What does their ideal future look like where this problem is solved? How would they look and feel? When they imagine this future, what else is true about their life? These become your "future pacing" copy elements.

**Q4: What Are Their Victories and Failures?**
What have they tried before? What small wins have they had? What crushing failures? The failures become empathy builders ("I know you've tried everything..."). The victories become proof that change is possible ("Remember when you...").

**Q5: What Outside Forces Do THEY Believe Have Prevented Their Best Life?**
Markets often have external villains: big pharma, the food system, environmental toxins, the government, the beauty industry, social media. Knowing these lets you build rapport: "I know you feel the deck is stacked against you..." These become corruption angles.

**Q6: What Are Their Prejudices?**
Not about discrimination. This is about biases and snap judgments. Example: Women 45+ have a prejudice against young beauty counter reps who "don't understand what it's like." Biz-opp prospects have a prejudice against gurus who flaunt Lamborghinis. Knowing these helps you avoid triggering rejection and lean into trust signals.

**Q7: Sum Up Their Core Beliefs About Life, Love, and Family In 1-3 Sentences.**
This is the deepest layer. What do they fundamentally believe about how the world works? This informs your entire brand voice and emotional appeal. Example: "She believes she's earned the right to feel beautiful. She's given so much to everyone else - her family, her career - and now it's her turn."

### Section B: Other Existing Solutions

**Q8: What is the Market Already Using? (List Out)**
Every competing solution they've tried, are currently using, or are aware of. Include both direct competitors and alternative approaches (e.g., for eye aging: eye creams, retinol, Botox, fillers, laser treatments, makeup tricks, doing nothing).

**Q9: What Has Their Experience Been Like?**
For each solution - what was the reality vs the promise? Where did it fall short?

**Q10: What Does the Market Like About Existing Solutions?**
What works. What they'd keep if they could. This tells you what table-stakes features your product must have.

**Q11: What Does the Market Dislike About Existing Solutions?**
The frustrations, the letdowns, the annoyances. These become comparison angles: "Unlike [solution], which [frustration], our approach..."

**Q12: Are There Horror Stories About Existing Solutions?**
These are gold for copy. Real stories of things going wrong - bad reactions, wasted money, embarrassing moments, health scares. Example: A Reddit story about a woman whose Botox went wrong and she couldn't move her face for months. These can power entire ad hooks.

**Q13: Does the Market Believe Existing Solutions Work? If Not, Why?**
This reveals the "efficacy paradox" - in many markets, people keep buying solutions they don't fully believe in. They declare "no cream can remove wrinkles" yet keep buying creams. They're not looking for another fantasy - they're searching for the one credible exception. Your job is to position your product as that exception.

### Section C: Curiosity Angles

These questions are designed to surface unique, compelling stories and historical angles that can power hooks, leads, and entire ad concepts.

**Q14: Has Someone Tried to Solve the Market's Pain Points Before In A Very Unique Way? What Was The Result?**
Look for unusual, forgotten, or suppressed attempts to solve the same problem. The more unexpected, the better.

**Q15: Is There A Conspiratorial Story Behind Why Old Solutions Didn't Work? Are There Any Older Attempts to Solve the Problem (Pre-1960) That Are Unique?**
Were they successful but forgotten? Were they suppressed? Why did they disappear?

Examples of strong curiosity angles:
- Nikola Tesla in the energy space: Big energy didn't want his solutions, he was discredited and shamed. His inventions were thrown into the ash heap of history until now.
- U.S. Army tried to cure foot fungus during WWII. The surgeon general was on a desperate race because troops were missing service time. They finally succeeded using Undecylenic Acid. But today we've forgotten how effective it is.
- Madame du Recamier, a 19th-century French socialite whose beauty secret was later commercialized by Harriet Hubbard Ayer - a "forgotten secret" angle.
- Bear extract (uva ursi) as a "forgotten ingredient" that naturally produces hydroquinone for skin lightening.

These angles become hooks: "This WWII-era discovery is the secret weapon dermatologists don't want you to know about..."

### Section D: Corruption Angles

These questions surface the market's existing distrust and channel it toward your product's positioning.

**Q16: Is There A Belief That the Market's Pain Point Used To Not Exist, Or Used To Not Be So Bad? Is There A Belief That It's Been Recently Exacerbated By Outside Forces? If So, What Are Those Forces?**

Pattern: "This isolated group of people doesn't struggle from [condition] that most of us do. In [country] we DO suffer from this. The reason why is that we are exposed to [outside forces] while this isolated group isn't."

Examples of strong corruption angles:
- Obesity and diabetes being the result of Dr. Ancel Keys and the sugar industry's cover-up of dietary fat research
- The beauty industry putting known toxins and carcinogens in products that actually make skin problems worse
- The food system and its role in inflammation, gut damage, and skin degradation
- The Separa tribe: perfect skin until petroleum companies brought processed food to their region
- Royal Raymond Rife: inventor whose cure was suppressed by the medical establishment

Corruption angles validate the market's existing skepticism ("We know you're skeptical - you have every right to be") and then position your product as the transparent, science-backed exception to an industry built on broken promises.

## 3. Primary Promises Framework

Primary promises are the specific, vivid outcomes your market wants to hear. They are NOT product features. They are the RESULT of using the product, described in the language the market uses.

### Structure

For each promise, capture:
1. **The promise itself** - stated in the customer's language
2. **The pain point it addresses** - the specific frustration this resolves
3. **The desired future state** - what life looks like when this promise is fulfilled (how they look, feel, and what else becomes true)

### How to Generate Primary Promises

Pull from the psychographic research:
- What are the specific pain points? (Each one implies a promise: "no more [pain point]")
- What are their hopes and dreams? (Each one implies a promise: "finally [dream]")
- What language do they use to describe the ideal outcome?
- What would make them feel the problem is truly solved?

### Example (from a different niche - weight loss supplement):
- Experience rapid fat loss from belly, hips, thighs without extreme diet or exercise
- Boost metabolism and burn fat 24/7
- Increase confidence, self-esteem, feeling attractive and vibrant again
- Fit back into favorite clothes like jeans, dresses, and swimwear
- Regain independence and stop feeling held back by weight
- Be admired, desired, and turn heads again
- Reclaim energy, youthfulness and love life again
- Free yourself from guilt, shame and worry about your weight

Notice: these are NOT ingredient claims or feature descriptions. They're emotional outcomes described in the market's own language.

## 4. Competitor Research Framework

For each competitor, capture ALL of the following. Do not stop at surface-level observations. Dig into their actual language.

### Per-Competitor Data Points

1. **Brand name and URL**
2. **Target demographic they speak to** - who do their ads and landing pages address? Age, gender, psychographic profile.
3. **Primary acquisition funnels** - how do they get new customers? Meta ads, Google, influencer partnerships, organic social, email, affiliate? What's the dominant channel?
4. **Core messaging** - what is their main promise? What headline do they lead with on their landing page? What's the first thing they say in their ads?
5. **Recurring hooks/angles/big ideas** - what themes keep appearing across their ads? What angles do they return to again and again? (These are their proven winners.) List at least 3 specific hooks.
6. **Pricing structure** - hero product price, bundles, subscriptions, discounts, free trial offers, money-back guarantee terms.
7. **Customer love** - what do reviewers praise? What specific benefits do they mention? Include at least 3 actual quotes from reviews or social media.
8. **Customer hate** - what do reviewers complain about? What specific frustrations surface? Include at least 3 actual quotes.
9. **Estimated revenue** - if findable via public data, industry reports, or tools like SimilarWeb.
10. **Specific repeated language** - exact phrases, claims, and verbiage they use consistently. These reveal what the market responds to. Example: if every competitor says "clinically proven", that's table stakes. If only one says "dermatologist-formulated", that might be an ownable angle.

### Cross-Competitor Analysis (After All Individual Profiles)

- **Positioning map** - where does each competitor sit relative to the others? (Price vs. positioning, science-backed vs. lifestyle, clinical vs. natural)
- **Common industry language** - phrases every competitor uses (these are table stakes your brand must match)
- **Differentiable language** - phrases only 1-2 competitors use (these could be ownable)
- **Messaging gaps** - what is NO competitor saying that the market wants to hear?
- **Opportunities** - angles, claims, or positions this brand could own
- **Format patterns** - what ad formats dominate? (UGC, talking head, before/after, lifestyle, clinical)

## 5. Research Depth Standards

These are non-negotiable minimums for every research phase. If a phase doesn't meet these standards, it needs another pass.

| Phase | Minimum Depth |
|-------|--------------|
| Market Awareness | Percentage breakdown for all 5 stages with reasoning. Final assessment with creative implications. |
| Competitor Research | At least 5 direct competitors. Each with all 10 data points filled. At least 3 real customer quotes per competitor. |
| Psychographic Research | All 16 questions answered with evidence. At least 20 real customer quotes from forums/social/reviews. At least 3 curiosity angles. At least 2 corruption angles. |
| Descriptive Language | At least 15 quotes describing pain points. At least 15 quotes describing desired outcomes. Adjectives and descriptors catalogued. |
| Customer Voice (Reviews) | At least 5 themes identified. At least 5 real quotes per theme. Recurring language patterns listed. |
| Ingredient/Science Research | Every ingredient covered. At least 2 studies per ingredient from NCBI/peer-reviewed sources. Claims focused on mechanism support and pain point relief. |
| Unified Research Document | All sections populated with synthesized insights. No generic filler. Every claim backed by evidence from the research phases. |

### Multi-Pass Research Rules

1. **Never do one search and call it done.** For each topic, run at least 3 different searches with different search terms.
2. **Search specific sources.** Don't rely on general web search. Explicitly search: Reddit (relevant subreddits), Trustpilot, Amazon reviews, forums, Quora, YouTube comments, Mumsnet (UK brands).
3. **Preserve exact language.** When you find a good customer quote, keep it exactly as written. Do not paraphrase or clean it up. The raw language is the value.
4. **After each phase, review and gap-fill.** Ask yourself: "What questions from the framework haven't been answered? What's still vague or generic?" Then do targeted searches to fill those gaps.
5. **Look for surprises.** The most valuable research findings are the ones that contradict your assumptions or reveal something unexpected about the market. Flag these prominently.
