# Principles, Rules, and Prohibitions

**Purpose:** The guardrails. What makes NSY scripts work, what breaks them, and what the skill must never do. Read this file BEFORE writing any script.

---

## THE 10 NSY WRITING RULES

Extracted from 27 winning scripts. These are not theory. These are patterns from scripts that actually converted.

**1. Open with borrowed attention. Never open with the product.**
Celebrity names, shocking stats, relatable problems, news events. The hook's job is to stop the scroll. The product appears later. ZAP4 opens with Bezos and Dana White. The product isn't mentioned until the 50% mark.

**2. Mirror the viewer's inner monologue.**
Use the exact language they use to describe their problem. Not marketing language. Customer language. "If you've tried every supplement on Amazon and got nothing but expensive piss" is customer language. "If you're looking for a natural testosterone solution" is marketing language. The first version makes the viewer think "that's exactly what happened to me." The second makes them think "this is an ad."

**3. Educate before you sell.**
The best-performing NSY scripts teach something the viewer didn't know. A hidden mechanism, a surprising cause, a timeline of what happens inside their body. Education earns attention and builds trust. Then the product becomes the tool that makes the education actionable. If the viewer learned something, they'll watch to the end.

**4. Introduce the product like a friend's recommendation, not a pitch.**
"That's when I found [product]." "It's called [product], and it works by..." "That's exactly why I created [product]." Never "Introducing [product], the revolutionary new..." The product enters through a door the script has already opened. The viewer should feel like they discovered it, not like it was sold to them.

**5. Stack specifics. Numbers, names, percentages, timeframes.**
"18 grams of protein - that's more than two eggs." "14 research-backed ingredients." "Over 2,000 verified reviews." "Only 84p per bowl." Specifics are more believable than vague claims at any length. The more specific, the more credible. Every number you can include is a trust deposit.

**6. Handle objections inside the story, not as a separate section.**
"And because so many men have been scammed by low-dosage junk from China that doesn't work, every bottle comes with a 90-day guarantee." The objection (scammed before), the proof (90-day guarantee), and the empathy (we know what you've been through) are all in one sentence, woven into the narrative. Never bolt on an FAQ section. Weave the objection handling into the flow.

**7. Always include risk reversal.**
The guarantee is not fine print. It is a selling argument. Position it as proof of confidence: "Feel the difference, or you pay nothing." "Try it for 30 days. If you don't love it, every penny back." In nearly every T1 script, the guarantee appears in the final quarter as the argument closer. It's the reason to act, not a legal disclaimer.

**8. Match the voice to the audience.**
A script for mums sounds different from a script for male gym-goers. Sentence length, vocabulary, emotional register, cultural references. A nurse talks differently from a UFC fighter. Contractions, regional dialect, sentence fragments, and direct questions to the viewer are all strategic tools. If the voice doesn't match who you're talking to, the viewer feels it instantly and scrolls.

**9. Write the hook last, iterate it most.**
The hook is the highest-leverage variable. A 10% improvement in hook retention means 10% more people see the mechanism, the proof, the CTA, everything. The body script can stay constant. The hooks get 4 variations. Each variation should test a strategically different approach, not minor wording changes. Four hooks that are slight rewrites of each other are one hook, not four.

**10. Keep it short enough to feel like content, long enough to sell.**
30-60 seconds is the sweet spot for Meta/TikTok cold traffic. If you can say it in 45 seconds, don't stretch to 90. If you need 90 seconds to build the argument (like SUR1's parental fear cascade), take 90. Length serves the argument. Never pad. Never rush. The script should feel like it's exactly as long as it needs to be.

## VOICE AND TONE PRINCIPLES

NSY scripts sound like a specific person talking to a specific person. Never "an ad."

**What to do:**
- Use contractions (it's, don't, you're, they're)
- Use sentence fragments when they feel natural in speech
- Use direct questions to the viewer when genuine ("You know that feeling when...")
- Use regional dialect when appropriate for the audience
- Use profanity when it's authentic to the voice ("got nothing but expensive piss")
- Vary sentence length naturally. Mix longer flowing sentences with shorter ones. Read it aloud - if it sounds rhythmically robotic, rewrite it.
- Let some sentences trail off naturally or connect with "and" like real speech
- Use active voice. The subject does the thing. "Magnesium helps your brain switch off" not "Your brain is helped to switch off by magnesium."
- One idea per sentence. If a sentence contains two ideas, make it two sentences.
- Short sentences create pace and urgency. Longer sentences create flow and explanation. Alternate based on what the moment needs.

**What to never do:**
- NEVER use staccato sentence fragments in a row ("No spike. No crash. Keeps you full until lunch."). This is the single most recognizable AI copywriting pattern. Real people don't talk in punchy three-word fragments back to back. If you catch yourself writing three short fragments in a row, rewrite it as a natural sentence.
- NEVER use rhetorical "direct questions" as transitions ("How'd they do it?" / "So what's the secret?" / "But here's the question..."). This is another dead giveaway of AI-generated scripts. Real people don't narrate their own curiosity. They just say the thing. If you need a transition, use a natural bridge like "Turns out..." or just state the next point.
- NEVER use rhetorical questions that sound like copywriting formulas. "What if I told you there was a better way?" belongs in a 2015 webinar. Not in an NSY script.
- NEVER write sentences that sound like they belong in a press release, a brochure, or a brand manifesto. If a sentence could appear on a corporate website without edits, it fails the anti-ad test.

**The anti-ad instruction:** Every NSY brief contains some version of "don't make this sound like an ad." The skill should internalize this. If a line sounds like it belongs in a commercial, rewrite it until it sounds like a real person talking to a friend.

## QUALITY CALIBRATION RULES

These are not rigid formulas. They're calibration tools. Apply with judgment.

**1. Specificity over vagueness.**
Statistics and specific numbers add credibility. "Your Fitbit score goes from 62 to 85" lands harder than "your sleep improves." Not every single claim needs a number attached, but when you have a specific stat, name, comparison, or timeframe available from the research docs, use it. Vague claims are forgettable. Specific claims stick.

**2. Fewer, harder points.**
AI loves to be comprehensive and list every possible benefit. Resist this. A script should make 2-3 arguments that hit hard, not 5-6 that bounce off. The exact number scales with length - a 45-second script can support 2 core points, a 90-second script can support 3-4. But the principle holds: cut the weakest points and give the survivors more room to breathe.

**3. Know when to go deeper on science.**
Mechanism depth depends on awareness level and market sophistication. For a Problem Aware audience hearing about magnesium for the first time, "it helps your brain switch off" is enough. For a Solution Aware audience who has already tried magnesium and it didn't work, you need GABA receptors, NMDA blocking, why glycinate absorbs differently. Read the market awareness doc for the client and calibrate accordingly.

**4. Be assertive, not reckless.**
AI defaults to hedge language - "might notice," "could help," "may experience." This makes scripts sound weak and uncertain. Be more assertive: "the bloating disappears," "you sleep through the night," "your energy comes back." BUT - stay compliant. Never claim the product cures, treats, or prevents any condition. When in doubt, use the language real customers use in their reviews. They're naturally assertive about their experience without making medical claims.

**5. Feature-benefit threading.**
Every product feature in a script must be threaded to a benefit in the same sentence. The pattern: [Technical thing they don't care about] that does [benefit they care about].

Good: "250mg magnesium glycinate - the form that actually crosses the blood-brain barrier and helps your brain switch off."
Bad: "Contains 250mg magnesium glycinate." (so what?)

Good: "Individual sachets - so you never have to measure and the ritual feels intentional."
Bad: "Individual sachet format." (why should I care?)

Features alone are boring. Benefits alone lack credibility. Thread them together.

## THE SELL THE CLICK VS. SELL THE PRODUCT FRAMEWORK

This changes the entire script ending and what information the script contains. Get it wrong and the ad works against the landing page instead of with it.

### Sell the Click (sending traffic to VSL or advertorial)
- Do NOT name the product in the ad
- Tease a breakthrough, solution, ritual, or method ("7 Second Morning Ritual")
- CTA = "watch this free presentation" or "tap below to learn more"
- The ad's only job is getting them to click
- The landing page sells the product
- The script can be shorter and more curiosity-driven

### Sell the Product (sending traffic to product page)
- Name the product in the ad
- Show features, benefits, transformation
- CTA = "click the link and get yours" or "grab yours now"
- The ad must do enough selling that the product page only needs to close
- Include offer details if space allows
- The script does the heavy lifting

### How to decide
- Client has a long-form VSL or advertorial funnel? Sell the click.
- Client sends traffic to a Shopify product page? Sell the product.
- If unsure? Ask the strategist where the traffic is going. Never guess.

### The Inverse Relationship
There's an inverse relationship between how much the ad sells and how much the landing page needs to sell.
- Short ad + minimal selling = needs long advertorial or detailed PDP downstream
- Long video ad + heavy selling = short product page just closes the deal
- Short curiosity ad = long VSL downstream converts

When the landing page is short, the script does heavy lifting. When the landing page is long, the script can be shorter and more curiosity-driven. Ask about the landing page before writing.

## WHAT NSY ADS MUST NEVER DO

### Structure violations
- **Never zigzag.** Every sentence advances the argument in one direction. No going back to the problem after introducing the solution. The viewer's mind has moved forward. Pulling them back breaks the spell.
- **Never include a lead section.** NSY ads are in-feed. The hook IS the lead. No fascination stacking. No loop-opening sequences. No "in the next 30 seconds, you'll discover..." preambles.
- **Never write an on-page VSL structure for in-feed ads.** Long-form sales letter structure feels like a sales pitch in a social feed. It gets scrolled past instantly.
- **Never write VSL-style copy for Meta ads.** NSY ads are short-form content pieces, not video sales letters. No "so to recap" summaries. No "but wait, there's more." No extended close sequences. No stacking of 5+ benefits in a list. No urgency countdowns. No "listen carefully" commands. The ad should feel like it belongs on someone's feed between a friend's holiday photos and a cooking video. If it feels like a pitch, it's wrong.
- **Never skip steps in the sequence.** Hook, Problem, Mechanism, Product, Proof, CTA. In that order. Always. The sequence exists because each step primes the next.

### Mechanism violations
- **Never use a mechanism that fails the simplicity test.** If you cannot explain it in one sentence to a friend at dinner and have them go "oh, that makes sense," it's too complex for a 45-second ad. If you need five studies to make it believable, the mechanism isn't simple enough.
- **Never present the mechanism as a lecture.** It should feel like a friend sharing a discovery, not a scientist giving a presentation. Education, not lecture.
- **Never keep a bad analogy just because it was generated.** The analogy must be intuitive and vivid. If it does not land in one hearing, rewrite it. "Like turning down the water pressure to fix a clogged pipe" works. "Like resynchronizing the oscillatory patterns of your neuronal networks" does not.

### Copy violations
- **Never use jargon without immediately explaining it.** "Your fibroblasts are shutting down" means nothing to the viewer. "The tiny cells that keep your skin tight are shutting down" works. Every technical term needs a plain-language translation in the same sentence.
- **Never use banned phrases.** "This changed my life," "this is a game changer," "revolutionary," "I love this product." Go 10 layers deeper. What specifically changed? How? What was the moment? Generic enthusiasm is the weakest form of testimonial.
- **Never use marketing language when customer language exists.** The customer says "I finally stopped feeling like a balloon after every meal." The brand says "improves gut microbiome diversity." Always use the customer version.
- **Never write closed-loop fascination bullets as hooks.** Write fragments. Open-ended. The first half of a puzzle, not the complete puzzle. A hook that answers its own question gives the viewer no reason to keep watching.

### Selling violations
- **Never reveal pricing in a Meta ad script.** The landing page handles pricing. The script sells the click or the product, not the package.
- **Never try to sell the bundle in a 45-second ad.** That's the funnel's job. The ad sells one thing: the reason to click or the reason to want the product.
- **Never delay the CTA to pack in more selling.** There is one CTA and it arrives at the natural peak. After the CTA, the argument is over. Don't keep talking.
- **Never assume the viewer has watched other content.** Every script stands alone. No "as you know" or "remember when I showed you." Each ad is the first time this person has seen the brand.

### Format violations
- **Never write fewer than 4 hooks.** Minimum is 4 hook variations. Each variation should test a strategically different approach.
- **Never write 4 hooks that are minor wording variations of the same idea.** Each must test a different hook type, psychological trigger, or entry point. Four hooks that say "my sleep was terrible" in slightly different words are one hook.
- **Never skip the dismiss-alternatives beat in health/wellness scripts.** Even one sentence attacking the obvious self-help solution is worth its weight in conversion rate. The viewer's first thought after the mechanism is "can I just fix this myself?" Close that exit door.

## CLIENT FEEDBACK PATTERNS (Common Revision Requests Decoded)

Real client feedback rarely tells you the exact problem. Here's what each common complaint actually means and how to fix it.

**"It sounds too salesy."**
What they mean: marketing language crept in. The script sounds like an ad, not a person. Rewrite in customer voice. Replace any line that sounds like it belongs in a brochure with the way a real customer would say it. Run the entire script through the anti-ad test.

**"This isn't us."**
What they mean: the voice doesn't match the brand. The tone, the energy, the cultural references feel off. Go back to the client research docs and check the brand voice section. Look at how the brand talks on their own social channels. The script needs to sound like it came from inside their world, not outside it.

**"Too long."**
What they mean: the script is padding. Not every "too long" note means "cut 15 seconds." It usually means some sections aren't earning their time. Apply the compression hierarchy. Cut the weakest points. Let the survivors breathe. The goal isn't a shorter script - it's a script where every second pulls its weight.

**"I don't get the connection."**
What they mean: the mechanism logic has a gap. The viewer (or the client) can't follow the chain from problem to cause to solution. There's a missing bridge somewhere. Re-read the mechanism section and ask: does each sentence lead logically to the next? Is there an assumption the viewer won't have? Fill the gap.

**"Can we add more benefits?"**
What they mean: usually the wrong instinct, but check first. If the proof section is thin (one benefit, no specifics), they might be right that it needs more substance. But if the script already has 2-3 solid points, adding more will make each one weaker. Push back gently: "We could add more, but the current benefits will land harder with room to breathe. Want me to strengthen the existing proof instead?"

## THE CONGRUENCY PRINCIPLE

The setting, lighting, creator energy, and context in the ad should match when and where the product is actually used in real life. This is subconscious but powerful - it creates alignment between the viewer's mental model of using the product and what they see on screen.

**Good congruency:**
- Bedtime supplement filmed in a cosy evening kitchen with warm lighting
- Morning vitamin filmed in bright natural kitchen light
- Skincare product filmed in a real bathroom mirror
- Gym recovery product filmed post-workout
- Wine replacement filmed in the exact setting someone would normally pour wine

**Bad congruency:**
- Bedtime supplement filmed in a bright studio
- Skincare product filmed at a desk
- A "morning routine" product filmed at night
- A relaxation product with high-energy music and fast cuts

When a hook changes, the body must adjust to match. If Hook 1 opens in a kitchen and Hook 2 opens in a gym, the body footage needs to support both contexts. Testing showed that congruency between hooks and body content increased hit rate from 8% to 20%.

For production notes in creator briefs, always specify the setting and why it matters. The setting is not decoration. It is part of the persuasion.

## THE STRAIGHT LINE PRINCIPLE

The most important structural rule. Every sentence advances the viewer from Point A (has the problem, doesn't know the solution) to Point B (understands the solution, ready to act) in a straight line.

No zigzagging. No detours. No circling back.

- Hook goes straight to Problem/Agitation
- Problem/Agitation goes straight to Mechanism
- Mechanism goes straight to Product
- Product goes straight to Proof
- Proof goes straight to CTA

Every sentence advances the argument in one direction. If a sentence doesn't advance, cut it. In a 45-second ad, one zigzag and the viewer is gone. They've mentally moved past the problem. Taking them back there feels repetitive and breaks the momentum.

The straight line applies within sections too. The problem section should escalate, not circle. The proof section should build, not repeat. Each sentence earns its place by taking the argument one step further.

## THE COMPRESSION HIERARCHY

When compressing from longer to shorter, sections disappear from the outside in. The mechanism is the last thing to go.

### What ALWAYS survives (even at 30 seconds)
- Hook (one line, creates the gap)
- Authority establishment (one sentence: "I'm a nurse" or "Board-certified endocrinologist, 18 years")
- Mechanism name + one analogy ("Your body's just lacking the raw materials" or "like turning down the water pressure to fix a clogged pipe")
- Dismiss alternatives (one sentence attacking the obvious self-help solution)
- One proof point with a specific number
- Solution tease or product name (depending on destination)
- CTA

### What disappears first (cut these before cutting anything else)
- Extended personal story / background narrative
- Named testimonials with backstories
- Individual ingredient breakdowns with clinical stats
- Price anchoring against multiple alternatives
- Pricing, guarantee details, bonus stacking
- Multiple CTAs
- FAQ / objection handling section
- Future pacing timelines (week-by-week)
- Product development / founder struggle narrative

### The key insight
The mechanism does not get cut. It gets compressed. Even at 30 seconds, name the problem mechanism and deliver the analogy. One good analogy can replace an entire mechanism explanation:
- "Like turning down the water pressure to fix a clogged pipe" (salt and blood pressure)
- "Like adding more cars to a traffic jam" (fiber and constipation)
- "Like putting them on a rollercoaster they can't get off" (sugar and kids' blood sugar)

At 90 seconds, the mechanism gets 2-3 sentences. At 45 seconds, 1-2 sentences. At 30 seconds, one sentence. But it is ALWAYS there.

## THE TWO-PROMISE ARCHITECTURE

### Two types of promises

**Teaser Promises:** Sell the VIDEO. Make the viewer want to keep watching. Tease but do not deliver. These become the HOOK.
- "There's a hidden reason your eye cream doesn't work"
- "What if the breakfast you're giving your kid is dissolving their brainpower?"

**Bold Product Claims:** Sell the PRODUCT. Make the viewer want to buy. Promise specific results. These become the PROOF section and CTA.
- "18 grams of protein per bowl - that's more than two eggs"
- "39% wrinkle reduction in 4 weeks"

### Why this split matters

Without it, scripts blend the two promise types. The hook tries to sell the product ("Get 39% fewer wrinkles!"). The proof section sounds like a teaser ("There's something special about this cereal..."). Everything sounds the same. The argument has no shape.

With it, each section has its own job:
- **Hook** uses teaser promises (curiosity, gap-creating)
- **Problem/Agitation** uses customer pain points
- **Solution** uses the mechanism
- **Proof/Demo** uses bold product claims + studies + testimonials
- **CTA** uses product promises + risk reversal

### The most common AI script failure

When you ask an LLM to "write a 45-second ad" without separating these promise types, it puts product claims in the hook (because they sound impressive) and teaser language in the product section (because it doesn't know what the product actually delivers). The separation is what gives the script its shape.

## THE UMP/UMS MECHANISM FRAMEWORK

Every health, wellness, and supplement script should have a mechanism built from two parts.

### UMP: Unique Mechanism behind the Problem
Reveals the REAL reason the viewer has been failing. Fills a knowledge gap. Makes them think "that explains everything."

A good UMP reframes why past solutions didn't work, introduces a concept the viewer was missing, and creates an emotional shift from frustration to understanding.

### UMS: Unique Mechanism behind the Solution
Shows HOW to fix the newly revealed problem. Must follow directly from the UMP. If the UMP says "the real problem is X," the UMS says "here is how you address X specifically."

### The three-part UMP pattern (for health/wellness scripts)

**Part 1: How the system is SUPPOSED to work.** Explain the natural process in simple terms. The viewer should nod along.

**Part 2: Why the system is NOT working.** Reveal the breakdown. This is the knowledge gap.

**Part 3: What external factors caused the malfunction.** Age, lifestyle, environment - something specific and personal.

At 60 seconds, all three parts get 1 sentence each. At 45 seconds, compress to parts 2 and 3 only (the viewer can infer part 1). At 30 seconds, compress to part 2 only with an analogy doing the work.

### The mechanism simplicity test
If you cannot hear it once or twice and then go tell someone else about it in 30 seconds, it is too complicated. Imagine explaining the mechanism to your partner at dinner. Can you do it in a few sentences and have them go "oh, that makes sense"? If you need five studies to make it believable, the mechanism is not simple enough.

## THE SOFT CTA PRINCIPLE

Hard CTAs ("BUY NOW!", "ACT FAST!") trigger ad blindness on Meta and TikTok. The platform rewards content that feels native. The CTA should feel like a friend sharing a recommendation.

**Soft CTAs that work:**
- "I'll leave the link right here, they sell out quick"
- "Link's below if you wanna check it out"
- "Click below to see what the deal is"
- "Worth trying if you're dealing with this"
- "They do a free 5-night trial - you just cover postage"
- "Try it risk-free for 30 days"

**Hard CTAs that trigger ad blindness (never use):**
- "BUY NOW before it's too late!"
- "ACT FAST - limited time offer!"
- "Don't miss out - click NOW!"
- "ORDER TODAY and save!"

The CTA should always include a REASON to act (a benefit, a risk reversal, or a specific next step), not just a direction. "Click the link below" is a direction. "Click below to try it free for 5 nights" is a CTA.

## SHOW DON'T TELL

Don't tell the viewer a claim. Show them.

"This watch is indestructible" is a claim. A hammer hitting the watch and it surviving is proof. "This product tastes amazing" is a claim. Someone's genuine first-taste reaction is proof. "This dissolves instantly" is a claim. A close-up of powder dissolving in water is proof.

**When writing visual direction, look for every claim in the script and ask: can we SHOW this?**

- "Gentle on your stomach" - show someone drinking it comfortably
- "Ready in 30 seconds" - show the real-time preparation, uncut
- "Tastes like raspberry lemonade" - show a genuine taste reaction
- "Your sleep score improves" - show an actual Fitbit/Garmin screen with the number

The visual direction in that row should demonstrate the claim physically while the creator or voiceover states it verbally. The viewer gets the claim through two channels simultaneously.

**Props and engineered demonstrations.** When the problem or benefit is hard to show directly, use props. A bidet brand used peanut butter on fabric to simulate the problem. A supplement brand could use a side-by-side dissolve test to show absorption difference. Suggest specific props or demonstrations in production notes that make the abstract concrete.

## THREE-LAYER HOOK DIFFERENTIATION

Every hook has three layers: text overlay, visual direction, and spoken audio. The critical rule: these three layers should NOT say the same thing.

Some people respond primarily to text. Some to visuals. Some to audio. Three different entry points means three chances to stop the scroll instead of one.

**BAD (all three layers repeat):**
- Text overlay: "My sleep was terrible"
- Visual: Creator looking tired in bed
- Audio: "My sleep was absolutely terrible"
- (Three chances to say the same thing = one chance)

**GOOD (each layer adds something different):**
- Text overlay: "Nobody warned me about this"
- Visual: Creator in evening kitchen, pushing wine glass aside, picking up sachet
- Audio: "Nobody warned me perimenopause would destroy my sleep"
- (Three different entry points = three chances to hook)

When writing hook options, always ensure the text overlay, visual direction, and spoken hook are complementary but NOT repetitive.

## UNEXPECTED VISUAL ELEMENTS

The highest-performing ads include small, unexpected visual details that trigger variable reward. Predictable content is boring. Content where you never know what's coming next keeps people watching.

This doesn't mean being random. It means including one or two visual moments the viewer doesn't expect:
- A visual metaphor (Mentos in soda bottles to show gut inflammation)
- A prop that's slightly unusual (supplement sachet next to a wine glass being pushed aside)
- A setting detail (steam rising from a mug, product spilled on a counter)
- A creator action that breaks pattern (pausing mid-sentence, holding up their phone to show a sleep score)
- A B-roll cut that's visceral or satisfying (powder dissolving in water, close-up of bubbles)

Always look for at least one moment per script that creates a micro-surprise. This is what separates ads that feel like content from ads that feel produced.

## THE MONEY SHOT PRINCIPLE

For any product with a visual transformation - skincare, hair, food, fitness, beauty tools - the most powerful visual demonstration should appear in the first 3 seconds. Not buried at second 32. Not teased and revealed later. The money shot IS the hook.

A hair tool ad showed the stunning before/after at second 32. By that point, most viewers had already scrolled past. The same shot at second 1 would have stopped every person who cares about hair.

**The test:** Does this product produce a visual result that, in a single frame, tells you what it does, who it's for, and why it matters? If yes, that frame is your hook.

When writing hook options, always consider whether a purely VISUAL hook (zero or minimal words, all visual) could outperform a copy-driven hook. For visual products, it often does.

---

## VISUAL RULE OF ONE

The Rule of One applies to production too. The B-roll, the setting, the creator's appearance, and the visual world of the ad should all signal WHO this ad is for.

A tactical watch ad showed the product's benefits but with generic B-roll that spoke to no one specific. A construction worker, a hiker, and a military veteran would all need completely different ads - different settings, different clothing, different scenarios.

**When writing visual direction, every visual choice should reinforce the target avatar:**
- Perimenopausal woman: real evening kitchen, warm lighting, cosy clothes
- Male runner: dawn, running shoes, Garmin, outdoor light
- Young wellness-curious woman: bright, clean, aesthetic apartment, modern kitchen

An ad where the copy targets one person but the visuals could be anyone is half an ad. The visuals must be as targeted as the words.

## VALENCE ZONES (Emotional Charge)

Most ad accounts live in negative, high-arousal territory - fear, anxiety, panic. Consciously choosing a different emotional register per concept opens audiences that fear-based scripts never reach.

**Zone 1 (Positive, Low Arousal):** Calm, cosy, supportive, peaceful. The evening ritual. The warm mug. Scripts in this zone feel like a deep exhale.

**Zone 2 (Positive, High Arousal):** Excitement, joy, empowerment, motivation. The transformation moment. The energy coming back. Scripts in this zone feel like a breakthrough.

**Zone 3 (Negative, Low Arousal):** Annoyance, frustration, disappointment, irritation. The "nothing works" exhaustion. Scripts in this zone feel like resigned fatigue.

**Zone 4 (Negative, High Arousal):** Panic, anxiety, loss, fear. The 3am wake-up. The health scare. Scripts in this zone feel like urgency and alarm.

When planning a batch, map which zone each concept lives in. If every concept is Zone 4, you're only reaching people who respond to fear. True creative diversity means covering multiple zones.

## SELF-CONCEPT ANCHORS

Three perspectives for how a script positions the viewer's identity.

**Actual Self:** Focuses on the problem as they experience it NOW. Heavy current-state agitation. "You're lying awake at 3am. Your mind won't stop."

**Ideal Self:** Focuses on the after-state. Aspirational. "Imagine waking up after 8 hours of unbroken sleep. Your mind is clear."

**Ought Self:** Told from a third person's perspective. "My wife hasn't slept properly in months. I found this for her." Motivated by duty, care, love. Reaches gift buyers and family members.

When writing hook variations, test across self-concept anchors, not just across different pain points.

## COGNITIVE BIAS AND HOW IT SHAPES THE SCRIPT

The cognitive bias determines WHAT you emphasize. Each bias produces a structurally different ad.

**Social Proof** ("If others like me did this, it's safer"): Review quotes, customer counts, and "people like you" language woven throughout. Trust built by volume.

**Risk/Loss Aversion** ("I want to avoid the wrong choice"): Education-heavy setup. Guarantees and honest downside framing prominent. Longer problem agitation.

**Authority** ("If an expert endorses this, it's credible"): Expert voice or referenced authority. Data points and clinical language (explained simply). One sentence of credentials changes everything after it.

**Effort Minimization** ("I want the least friction"): Language focused on simplicity: "takes 30 seconds," "one sachet," "mix and drink." Agitate the friction of alternatives.

**Novelty/Pattern Interrupt** ("This is different, pay attention"): Unexpected hooks, controversy, visual interruptions. Works better with younger audiences.

**Identity Reinforcement** ("This aligns with who I am"): Value-led messaging, community framing. "If you're the kind of person who..." qualifiers. Product becomes a signal of identity.

A social proof script and an authority script for the same product to the same avatar will look completely different. This is how you produce psychologically distinct ads, not just different wording.

## THE SAFETY LINE

When someone learns about a new health/supplement product, their first instinctive question is "is this safe for me?" Address it before they have to ask.

One sentence, placed right after product introduction or mechanism explanation.

**Examples:**
- "It's gentle on your stomach - no laxative effect like cheaper forms."
- "Zero caffeine, zero sugar, safe for nightly use."
- "Third-party tested. No fillers, no artificial sweeteners."

**When to include it:** Any health, wellness, or supplement script. For beauty and lifestyle products, include it when the product touches the body or when the audience skews older and more cautious.

**Where it goes:** After the product is introduced, before or during the proof section. One sentence. State it as fact, not as a defensive response.

---

## FLAG RE-HOOK MOMENTS

When writing a script, identify 2-3 moments in the body that could be tested as alternate hooks in future iterations. A reaction shot, a visual demonstration, a single powerful line.

In editor notes for those rows, add: "This moment could be tested as an alternate hook in a future iteration - make sure the visual is strong enough to work standalone."

This is how one winning ad becomes source material for 5-10 variations. The body stays the same. The hooks get swapped from moments flagged during the original script.

## PERSONA-TO-SCRIPT MAPPING

Each persona element feeds a specific section. This ensures every beat is anchored to something real.

| Persona Element | Feeds Script Section | How It Shows Up |
|---|---|---|
| Core motivator / desire | Hook (opens the curiosity loop) | The hook promises or implies what they want most |
| Core fear | Problem agitation (twists the knife) | The agitation names the fear specifically |
| Cognitive bias | Proof type and social proof placement | Determines HOW trust is built in the script |
| Awareness level | Script length, education depth, CTA directness | Problem aware = longer setup, softer CTA. Product aware = shorter, more direct |
| Valence zone | Emotional register and tone | Zone 1 = warm pacing. Zone 4 = urgency, faster pacing |
| Self-concept anchor | Script perspective and framing | Actual self = agitate pain. Ideal self = paint transformation. Ought self = third person |

Before writing, check each persona element and confirm you know which script section it feeds. If you can't connect a persona element to a beat in the script, either the persona is underdeveloped or the script isn't using it.

## THE DISMISS-ALTERNATIVES BEAT

After revealing the mechanism and before introducing the product, close the self-help exit door. The viewer's first thought after understanding the mechanism is: "OK, can I just fix this myself?"

"And all the $200 eye creams in the world won't fix this because they're targeting the wrong problem."
"That's why creams and serums can't fix it. They work on the surface. The problem is underneath."
"And no amount of sleep sprays, melatonin, or chamomile tea is going to replace the mineral your body is missing."

**The "Makes It Worse" upgrade:** The strongest version reveals that the current solution is not just failing but ACTIVELY making things worse.

"Wine doesn't just fail to help your sleep - it actively destroys your REM cycles and spikes your cortisol at 3am."
"Magnesium oxide doesn't just absorb poorly - the unabsorbed portion causes the stomach issues that make you stop taking it."
"Melatonin doesn't just stop working over time - it trains your brain to produce less of its own sleep hormone."

When the research supports it, always prefer the "makes it worse" version. It transforms the alternative from "doesn't work" (passive failure) to "is hurting you" (active harm). The motivation shifts from "I should try something better" to "I need to stop doing this immediately."

This beat is so important it survives compression when product build-up, credibility backstory, and parts of the close are eliminated.
