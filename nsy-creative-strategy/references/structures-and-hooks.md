# Script Structures, Hooks, Compression, and Modular Creative

**Purpose:** How to choose the right structure for each concept, build hooks that stop the scroll, compress scripts to any length, and think in modular scenes. Read AFTER script-examples.md (patterns first, then theory).

---

## THE FOUR NSY STRUCTURES

Every NSY script uses one of these four structures. The choice depends on creator type, awareness level, proof assets, and destination. Never blend structures. Pick one.

### Structure 1: Problem > Mechanism > Solution (The NSY Default)

**Use for:** 70%+ of health, wellness, beauty, and supplement scripts. Any product with a surprising mechanism.

**The sequence:**
1. Hook/Problem Callout (3-5 sec)
2. Problem Agitation (5-10 sec) - connect hook to daily pain using customer language
3. Mechanism Reveal (10-15 sec) - the hidden cause. 2-3 sentences max. Name it, analogize it, explain why current solutions fail
4. Product Introduction (5-10 sec) - product as the vehicle for the mechanism fix
5. Proof (10-15 sec) - one specific stat, testimonial, or demo
6. CTA with risk reversal (3-5 sec)

**When the mechanism is the selling point:** The mechanism creates differentiation. "Your testosterone didn't disappear. Your body's just lacking the raw materials." That one sentence is why ZAP4 converts.

**At 45 seconds, the mechanism compresses to:** Name the problem mechanism + one analogy + one sentence on why alternatives fail. Three sentences total.

**At 30 seconds, the mechanism compresses to:** Name it and analogize it. Two sentences. The analogy does the work of a full explanation.

**Creator type:** Expert/doctor spokesperson OR AI voiceover with B-roll. The mechanism needs credibility to land.

### Structure 2: Personal Discovery > Solution (The UGC Template)

**Use for:** Creator-filmed UGC content. Physical products, beauty tools, lifestyle products. Any product where personal experience is more persuasive than science.

**The sequence:**
1. Audience Hook + Life Change Promise (3-5 sec)
2. Problem Agitation as Personal Struggle (10-15 sec) - "I tried X, I tried Y, nothing worked"
3. Discovery Moment (3-5 sec) - "Then I found..." or "Then my friend told me about..."
4. Product Reveal + How It Works (10-15 sec) - product name, key features, how it solves
5. Personal Transformation (5-10 sec) - specific results, what changed
6. Offer + CTA (3-5 sec)

**The creator IS the proof.** No credentials needed. No mechanism needed. Their personal story carries the argument. "I'm just like you. I had the same problem. I found this thing. It changed my life."

**Creator type:** Everyday person UGC creator. Their relatability is their authority.

### Structure 3: Authority/Affinity + Life-Changing Discovery

**Use for:** Creators whose profession or lifestyle gives them credibility. The nurse, the athlete, the busy mum, the construction worker.

**The sequence:**
1. Contrarian Hook + Personal Authority (3-5 sec) - "Why I'll never [common solution] again. I'm a [profession]."
2. High-Stakes Situation (5-10 sec) - important event, couldn't afford failure
3. Switch from Old to New (5 sec) - "But one day I swapped my [old] for [new]"
4. Experiencing Benefits (10-15 sec) - primary impact + deeper transformation
5. Product Features + Authority Endorsement (5-10 sec) - "That's why I now recommend..."
6. Social Proof + Offer + CTA (5 sec)

**Creator type:** Someone whose daily experience makes them a credible endorser. Their profession is mentioned in the first 5 seconds.

### Structure 4: Contrarian + Expert Reveals Mechanism (The Sophisticated Play)

**Use for:** Products with strong science and expert spokesperson. Solution-aware audiences who need to understand why previous solutions failed. The most structurally sophisticated short VSL template.

**The sequence:**
1. Hook + Contrarian Twist (3-5 sec) - benefit promise + belief challenge
2. Authority Setup + Analogy (5-8 sec) - one sentence of credibility + THE analogy
3. Progressive Mechanism Reveal (15-25 sec) - how the system works, what goes wrong, body's failed compensation, why it escalates
4. Dismiss Alternatives (5 sec) - one sentence using the same analogy
5. Solution Introduction (5-10 sec) - authority + named method + how it works
6. Social Proof Story (5-10 sec) - one patient/customer transformation
7. CTA with Benefit Stack (5 sec)

**This structure preserves the most VSL logic.** It has a real mechanism section, a real dismiss-alternatives beat, and social proof. For brands with strong mechanisms, this is the structure that lets the mechanism do the selling.

**Creator type:** Expert/doctor spokesperson. AI voiceover with authority script.

## CHOOSING THE RIGHT STRUCTURE

Ask four questions. The answers point to one structure.

| Question | If the answer is... | Use... |
|---|---|---|
| **Creator type?** | Expert/doctor/AI voiceover | Structure 1 or 4 |
| | Peer/UGC creator (no credentials) | Structure 2 |
| | Creator with relevant profession | Structure 3 |
| **Awareness level?** | Problem-aware (know the pain, not the solution) | Structure 1 or 2 |
| | Solution-aware (comparing options) | Structure 4 |
| | Product-aware (evaluating this specific product) | Testimonial-heavy version of 2 or 3 |
| **Strongest proof asset?** | Strong mechanism with science | Structure 1 or 4 |
| | Strong testimonial library | Structure 2 or 3, stack social proof |
| | Strong before/after or demo | Structure 2 or 3, show transformation |
| **Destination?** | VSL or advertorial | Tease the solution. Don't name product. CTA = "learn the method" |
| | Product page (Shopify, DTC) | Name the product. Sell directly. CTA = "click the link and get yours" |

When in doubt, use Structure 1 for health/supplement brands and Structure 2 for physical products and beauty.

## BODY STRUCTURE ARCHETYPES (from NSY winners)

Beyond the four main structures, the body of the script tends to follow one of these pacing patterns:

### 1. Problem Timeline Cascade (60-120s)
Medical/health education angle. Opens with a systemic problem, escalates through a timeline of what happens if you ignore it. Each beat raises the stakes. Best for educated audiences and products with strong clinical data.

### 2. Conversational UGC Storytelling (45-75s)
Personal journey told in natural speech. No clinical language. Feels like a friend texting you about something they discovered. Skips around chronologically the way real people talk. Best for relatable creators and products where transformation matters more than mechanism.

### 3. Educational Authority (45-90s)
Expert or doctor perspective. Fact-heavy, but delivered with warmth rather than lecture energy. Uses stats and studies as proof, not as the hook. Best for supplements, skincare with clinical backing, and anything where "a doctor said so" matters.

### 4. Quick Hit / Format Piece (30-45s)
Short, punchy, visual-led. Minimal dialogue. Text overlays carry most of the messaging. Fast cuts. Works for ASMR, unboxing, side-by-side demos, and listicle-style formats. Best for retargeting and product-aware audiences who just need a nudge.

## SECTION PERCENTAGES (Time Allocation)

For a 45-second script:

| Section | % | Approx Time |
|---|---|---|
| Hook (replaces Lead) | 10% | ~4 sec |
| Problem/Agitation | 18% | ~8 sec |
| Solution/Mechanism | 20% | ~9 sec |
| Proof/Demo | 40% | ~18 sec |
| CTA (replaces Close) | 12% | ~6 sec |

**The mechanism is the argument.** Even in 45 seconds, it gets 20% of the time. This is not a section to cut. It gets compressed, never eliminated.

**Proof/Demo is the longest section.** This is where specifics, testimonials, and features do their work. At 40%, it carries the selling.

## THE COMPRESSION HIERARCHY

The most important concept for adapting scripts across lengths. When you compress a 90-second script to 45 seconds or 30 seconds, here is what stays and what goes.

### What survives at EVERY length (even 30 seconds):
- Hook (always)
- Authority signal (one sentence - a credential, a profession, a statistic)
- UMP name + one analogy (the mechanism never disappears, it just shrinks)
- Dismiss alternatives (one sentence - this survives compression better than almost anything else)
- One proof point with a specific number
- Solution tease or product name (depending on destination)
- CTA

### What disappears first (when cutting for time):
- Extended personal backstory or struggle narrative
- Named testimonials with detailed backstories
- Full ingredient breakdowns (compress to "key ingredient + what it does")
- Price anchoring and pricing math
- Guarantee details (compress to "money-back guarantee" mention in CTA)
- Multiple CTAs (keep the strongest one)
- FAQ-style objection handling
- Future pacing timelines ("imagine 6 months from now...")
- Founder origin story

### The key insight: Mechanism gets COMPRESSED, never cut

Even at 30 seconds, you name the problem mechanism and deliver the analogy. One good analogy replaces an entire mechanism explanation. A 90-second script might spend 25 seconds on the mechanism. At 30 seconds, you have 2 sentences. Those 2 sentences must contain: the mechanism name and the analogy. Everything else in the mechanism section is expendable.

## HOOK GENERATION

### The 7 Core NSY Hook Types (from 27 winning scripts)

1. **Timeline / "What happens if"** - "This is what would happen if you took Organised for 30 days"
2. **Celebrity name-drop** - "Jeff Bezos. Dana White. Both went from weak to absolutely jacked..."
3. **Symptom stack / "If you..."** - "If you're over 40 and you feel weaker than you ever have..."
4. **News/current event hijack** - "Trump just announced tariffs on braiding hair imports"
5. **Direct confession** - "I wasn't even sure if I should make this video, but..."
6. **"What I wouldn't do" / Negative framing** - "5 things I'd never eat as a doctor"
7. **Hidden danger / Revelation** - "What if the breakfast you're giving your kid every morning is actively dissolving their brainpower?"

### Additional Hook Frameworks

These come from broader direct response training and apply across all NSY scripts:

**Open Loops / Curiosity Gaps:** Create an information gap the viewer needs to close. The brain cannot tolerate an open pattern. Start a story, withhold the resolution. State a surprising fact, withhold the explanation.

**Juxtaposition:** Place two things in the same sentence that should not go together. The conflict creates an instant curiosity gap. "I quit the gym and lost 20 lbs." "I stopped buying expensive skincare and my skin got better." Different from contrarian hooks (which challenge a belief) - juxtaposition hooks combine two true things that sound contradictory.

**Ethical Fear:** "Watch out for these ingredients when buying shampoo." Protective framing. Positions the viewer as needing to defend themselves from something hidden. Works especially well in health/beauty where ingredient transparency matters.

**Belief Challenge:** "Most people think dandruff comes from a dry scalp, but it's actually the opposite." Directly contradicts a widely held assumption. The viewer stays to find out why they were wrong.

**Hormozi Value Equation:** Dream outcome x perceived likelihood / time delay x effort. The hook promises high dream outcome, high perceived likelihood, low time, and low effort. "39% wrinkle reduction in 4 weeks, no injections."

**Third-party credibility:** Lead with someone else's opinion. "My wife won't stop talking about this." Starting with a third party bypasses the "this feels like an ad" filter.

### The Three-Layer Hook System

Every hook should specify all three layers. They work together but can be tested independently:

1. **Text overlay:** What appears on screen (can be different from what is said)
2. **Visual direction:** What the viewer sees in the first 3 seconds
3. **Spoken/audio hook:** What the viewer hears

**CRITICAL: The three layers must NOT say the same thing.** Each layer is a separate shot at hooking a different type of viewer. Some people respond to text. Some respond to visuals. Some respond to audio. If all three layers express the identical message, you waste two shots. Each layer should complement the others while each being strong enough to stop a scroll independently.

### Hook Variation Strategy

Three hooks that are minor wording variations of the same idea waste two test slots. Each hook should test a different strategic approach:

**Option A - Different strategic angles:**
- Variation 1: Different problem/pain point (attack different frustration)
- Variation 2: Different format (question vs bold claim vs curiosity gap vs confession)
- Variation 3: Different persona (1st person vs 3rd person vs direct address)

**Option B - Different psychological dimensions:**
- Hook 1: Agitates a core desire
- Hook 2: Agitates a core fear
- Hook 3: Utilizes a key cognitive bias (social proof, authority, loss aversion)

### Hook Includes Problem Agitation Context

Hook variations are NOT just first-line swaps. Each hook variation should include a different problem agitation context. The first 10-15 seconds are unique per variant. The rest of the script converges on the same mechanism and solution.

This means a "hook variation" is really the first 2-3 lines of the script, not just the opening sentence.

### The Fragmented Hook Principle

NSY hooks should be fragments, not fascination bullets. Open-ended, not closed-loop. The first half of a puzzle, not the complete puzzle.

**Good:** "Jeff Bezos. Dana White. Both went from weak to absolutely jacked..." (opens a loop - HOW did they do it?)
**Bad:** "How two billionaires transformed their bodies using a natural testosterone stack" (closes the loop - nothing left to find out)

Never write closed-loop hooks. The hook's job is to create a gap, not fill one.

### The ADHD Super-Hook Framework

Highest-performing Meta ads pack multiple hooks into the first 30 seconds. Each opens a new curiosity gap without closing the previous one. Multiple speakers rapid-firing hooks with visual pattern interrupts between each. This is an advanced format that works when you have multiple creators or can cut between different footage sources.

The principle: Instead of one hook followed by a body, the first 30 seconds IS the hook. Every new beat re-hooks a viewer who was about to scroll away. By the time the body starts, three separate curiosity loops are open simultaneously.

### Trigger Events as Hook Fuel

What specific life event pushed someone from "I should do something" to "I'm buying this now"? Trigger events are seasonal and life-stage hooks that feel timely and personally relevant.

**Life-stage triggers:**
- Perimenopause onset ("When your sleep fell apart after 40...")
- A health scare or doctor's visit ("That appointment where they told you your bone density is dropping...")
- A milestone birthday ("I turned 50 and decided something had to change")
- A relationship change ("After the divorce, I stopped sleeping")

**Seasonal triggers:**
- January / Dry January ("This January, instead of quitting wine cold turkey...")
- Post-Christmas ("After two weeks of drinking every night, my body was screaming")
- Summer (bikini season for beauty, holiday energy for supplements)
- Winter ("The dark evenings are when the wine habit creeps back")

**Situational triggers:**
- A friend's recommendation ("My friend kept going on about this magnesium drink")
- Seeing an ad repeatedly ("I kept seeing this ad so I finally tried the free trial")
- A specific bad night ("Last Tuesday I woke up at 3am for the fifth night in a row")

When the research reveals common trigger events from reviews, ad comments, or post-purchase surveys, use them as hook openings. A hook built around a trigger event feels like it was made for the viewer at this exact moment in their life.

### Re-Hook Moments

Flag 2-3 body moments that could become alternate hooks. Every new beat in the script should give a reason to keep watching. If a line in the mechanism section could stop a scroll on its own, mark it as a potential hook for future variations.

## THE MODULAR CREATIVE FRAMEWORK

Ads are modular, not monolithic. Every NSY script should be built with six swappable scenes. This is what enables hook variation testing without rewriting the entire script, and it is what turns one winning ad into dozens of variations.

### The Six Scenes

| Scene | Function | Duration | What Varies |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scene 1: Intro/Hook | Stop scroll, spark curiosity | 3-5 sec | Different pain point, format, persona, or psychological trigger. Text + visual + audio are independently variable. |
| Scene 2: Problem Illustration | Show/tell the pain | 5-10 sec | Personal story vs statistic vs relatable scenario vs direct address |
| Scene 3: Failed Alternative | Dismiss obvious solution (why it fails) | 3-5 sec | Different alternative dismissed, different analogy |
| Scene 4: Our Solution | Introduce product/mechanism (why it's great) | 5-10 sec | Mechanism-led vs feature-led vs transformation-led |
| Scene 5: Social Proof / Authority | Demonstrate results | 10-20 sec | Testimonial vs before-after vs live demo vs authority endorsement |
| Scene 6: Offer + CTA | Soft close | 3-5 sec | Offer A (discount) vs Offer B (free shipping) vs Offer C (bonus stack) |

**Each scene is independently testable and swappable.** One winning ad becomes the source for dozens of variations by mixing and matching scenes.

**Scene 1 is the only scene that gets 4 variations per concept.** Scenes 2-6 stay constant. This is the NSY standard: main script + 4 hook variations = 5 total assets per concept.

## IN-FEED VS ON-PAGE STRATEGY

Two fundamentally different contexts that require different script approaches:

**In-Feed (the ad itself):**
- Education-forward, native to the feed
- No classic long-form lead
- Compressed story
- Curiosity throughout - every beat gives a reason to keep watching
- The ad's job is to create enough interest to click

**On-Page (landing page/VSL after click):**
- Heavy lead section
- Full mechanism depth with citations
- Complete close with objection handling, guarantee, and price justification

**NEVER take an in-feed style and use it on-page.** It will feel thin and unsupported.
**NEVER take an on-page structure and run it in-feed.** It will feel like a lecture and get scrolled past.

The in-feed ad sells the click. The on-page content sells the product.

## PRODUCT INTRODUCTION PATTERNS

The transition from problem/mechanism to product should never feel like a hard pivot. These are the patterns that work:

- **"But it doesn't have to be that way"** bridge - acknowledges the pain, opens the door
- **"Here's what I take/recommend"** personal endorsement - creator vouches directly
- **"That's why I started recommending"** professional endorsement - authority figure shifts from diagnosis to prescription
- **"What if you could get the same thing"** comparison pivot - links to something the viewer already values
- **Direct product education woven into the narrative** - no transition at all, the product appears naturally as part of the story

The worst version: "Introducing [Product Name]!" with no bridge. This screams ad and breaks immersion.

## THE DISMISS ALTERNATIVES BEAT

Pre-empt the viewer's "Can I just fix this myself?" thought. One sentence attacking the obvious alternative. This beat survives compression better than almost anything else because it directly addresses the objection that would stop someone from buying.

In health/wellness, the obvious alternatives are usually: diet alone, exercise alone, a cheaper supplement, doing nothing. Name the specific alternative and explain in one sentence why it does not work for THIS problem (using the mechanism you just established).

Non-negotiable in health/wellness scripts. If the viewer thinks they can solve this without buying, they will not buy.

## CTA PATTERNS

### Primary CTA Types
- **Guarantee-led** (most common in NSY winners) - "Try it risk-free with our 90-day money-back guarantee"
- **Urgency/scarcity** - "This batch is selling out" or seasonal deadline
- **Soft/conversational** - "I'll leave the link right here" (best for UGC)
- **Value stack with price anchoring** - "You'd pay $200 for a doctor's visit, this is less than $2 a day"

### Double CTA Strategy
Place the first CTA after the emotional sell (catches impulsive buyers). Place the second after the logical content - stats, features, guarantee (catches rational buyers). Two different buyer types, two different moments to convert them.

## CONCEPT FORMAT LIBRARY (Visual Execution Styles)

Structures tell you WHAT story to tell. Concept formats tell you HOW to visually execute it. One structure can be filmed using multiple concept formats. The strategist picks both a structure AND a concept format per brief.

### Core Concept Formats

**Credible Narrator:** Creator establishes authority in the hook (profession, title, credential) then delivers the script. Talking head with B-roll cuts. Best for health/wellness where authority matters.

**TikTok Comment Response:** TikTok comment sticker as the hook (addresses a key buyer objection). Video proceeds to clear the objection or tell a story. Feels native. Best for retargeting and objection-busting. Every FAQ from reviews can become one of these.

**Split Screen:** Two things shown simultaneously. Problem on one side, solution on the other. Or competitor vs your product. Best for comparison-driven concepts.

**ASMR / No Dialogue:** No speaking. Product interaction with amplified sounds - unboxing, pouring, mixing, applying. Text overlays carry messaging. Low production barrier. Best for products with a strong sensory experience.

**3rd Party POV:** Script told from someone else's perspective. "I bought this for my wife because she hasn't slept in months." Reaches gift buyers, partners, and family members. Unlocks a completely different audience.

**Podcast Style:** Creator filmed with podcast mic setup. Automatic authority signal from the environment. The mic setup makes even casual conversation feel credible.

**Founder Story:** Founder explains why they created the product. Limited use (1-2 per batch max). Most authentic when the founder has a personal connection to the problem.

**Man on the Street:** Someone approaches people in public and asks about the problem or product. Creates social proof through multiple reactions. Feels organic.

**Green Screen:** Creator filmed against green screen with supporting imagery - studies, product shots, competitor comparisons, before/after photos. Combines talking head authority with visual evidence.

**Single Testimonial:** One customer's full story. No mechanism explanation. The transformation IS the proof. Best when the testimonial is specific and emotionally compelling.

### How to Use This in Concepting

When developing a concept, specify both elements:
- **Structure:** Problem > Mechanism > Solution (Structure 1)
- **Concept Format:** Credible Narrator (pharmacist talking to camera)

The same structure can produce completely different ads by changing the concept format:
- Structure 1 + Credible Narrator = pharmacist explaining why glycinate absorbs better
- Structure 1 + ASMR / No Dialogue = close-up of sachet dissolving, text overlays carry the mechanism
- Structure 1 + Green Screen = creator with clinical studies behind them
- Structure 1 + TikTok Comment Response = answering "does magnesium actually help sleep?" with mechanism explanation

This is how you produce genuinely different creative from the same strategic foundation.

## FRAMEWORK QUICK-REFERENCE (NSY Wiki Frameworks)

The structures above are the skeleton. These frameworks from the NSY Wiki tell you which components to stack inside that skeleton:

| Framework | Best For | Key Components |
|---|---|---|
| PAS | Cold traffic, problem-aware | Problem > Agitate > Solution |
| AIDA | Aspirational/lifestyle angles | Attention > Interest > Desire > Action |
| Enemy | Solution-aware, comparing options | Shared value > Enemy identified > Hero (your product) |
| QUEST | Very specific avatar targeting | Qualify > Understand > Educate > Stimulate > Transition |
| Outcome | Transformation-focused | Tease promised land > Show difficulty > Product as vehicle |
| FOMO | Strong review counts, viral momentum | Social proof lead > More proof > Benefit > CTA |

**Key principle from the Wiki:** "Frameworks are NOT formulas. Formulas are restrictive. They obstruct the creative process. Frameworks, consisting of components that can be packaged together, are the right amount of structure and freedom for creativity."

## PERSONIFICATION CONCEPT FORMAT (AI Animation)

A distinct concept format where the problem is personified as a villain character, the product as a hero character, and the consumer as the victim. This format is always produced as AI animation (Seedance 2.0) because you can't film a talking beer glass or a supplement sachet with arms.

### How It Differs from the Enemy Framework
The Enemy framework (above) positions a common enemy conceptually - "the real problem isn't X, it's Y." Personification goes further: the enemy becomes a literal animated character with a body, voice, personality, and physical actions. It's not "alcohol is bad for your sleep" - it's a beer pint glass villain with thin arms and a dark cape, sucking magnesium out of a man's skull through a straw while taunting the camera.

### The Three Characters

| Character | Role | Visual Style | Voice |
|-----------|------|-------------|-------|
| Villain | The problem personified (alcohol, sugar, stress, junk food) | Menacing but Pixar-friendly. Thin limbs, cape, evil grin. | Sinister, smug, taunting. Speaks directly to camera in first person. |
| Victim | The consumer being exploited | Matches target audience (age, gender, appearance). Two states: defeated and restored. | Does not speak. Reacts physically. |
| Hero | The product personified | Matches actual product packaging. Muscular, cape, protective aura. | Warm, authoritative, reassuring. Same base voice as villain but different tone. |

### Script Structure for Personification

| Phase | Scenes | Speaker | What Happens |
|-------|--------|---------|-------------|
| Hook | 1 | Villain | Villain introduces self while exploiting victim. "I'm your nightly beer." |
| Escalation | 2-4 | Villain | Villain narrates ongoing damage. Each scene shows different harm to victim. |
| Peak | 5 | Villain | Damage at maximum. Villain triumphant. Victim at lowest point. |
| Turn | 6 | Hero | Hero arrives. INSTANT colour shift from dark to bright. Reverses damage. |
| Benefits | 7-9 | Hero | Hero explains what they do, physically shown on victim's body. |
| Social Proof | 10 | Hero | Scale of transformation. Villain rejected. |
| CTA | 11 | Hero | Product offer. Hero delivers to viewer. Fourth wall break. |

### Core Rule
The victim is present in EVERY scene (except social proof). The villain or hero is ALWAYS physically doing something TO the victim - not just standing nearby. This is NOT villain vs hero fighting each other. It's villain exploiting victim, then hero rescuing victim.

### Physical Interactions to Write Into Scripts

**Villain exploiting victim:**
- Draining (straw in head, hands on temples siphoning particles)
- Puppeting (strings on wrists, controlling movements)
- Weighing down (sitting on chest, pressing on shoulders)
- Poisoning (pouring liquid into mouth, injecting into veins)
- Suffocating (bag over head, smothering sleep)
- Fake friendship (arm around victim while secretly doing damage)

**Hero protecting victim:**
- Healing (hands on temples flowing green particles in)
- Shielding (energy shield projected over sleeping victim)
- Freeing (ripping off puppet strings)
- Guarding (standing beside bed as bodyguard)
- Placing product (putting product on bedside table for the viewer)

### When to Use Personification
- Health/wellness brands where the enemy is a substance (alcohol, sugar, caffeine)
- Products that counter a specific biological process (cortisol, inflammation, oxidative stress)
- Brands where the mechanism is "we do the opposite of X"
- When you want scroll-stopping impossible visuals that AI animation enables

### Example: HM36 (Healthy Metal)
- **Villain:** Beer pint glass with thin arms/legs, dark red cape, evil grin
- **Victim:** Middle-aged British man, 50s, beer drinker, can't sleep
- **Hero:** Healthy Metal sachet with muscular arms, cyan cape, gold aura
- **Hook:** Beer villain with straw jammed in man's skull, sucking blue magnesium particles: "I'm your nightly beer. I'm why you can't sleep... and you don't even know it."
- **Turn:** Sachet hero bursts into room, rips puppet strings off man's wrists. Instant colour shift from dark reds to warm cyan/gold.
- **CTA:** Hero places product on sleeping man's bedside table, turns to camera: "Free 5-night trial. Just cover shipping."
