# Persona Architecture

**Purpose:** The persona and audience targeting system. How to build psychologically distinct micro personas that produce genuinely different scripts, not surface-level variations of the same message.

**Source:** NSY creative strategy framework, cross-referenced with psychological advertising principles and real campaign performance data.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS FILE

Every script starts with a persona. Not a demographic profile ("women 25-45"), but a psychographic portrait that tells you exactly what to say, how to say it, and what format to say it in.

The system has four components. Each component answers a different creative question. Together, they produce a micro persona so specific that the script practically writes itself.

If you skip the persona work, you end up with scripts that sound the same. You get three hooks that all agitate the same fear with slightly different phrasing. You get body copy that educates when it should empathize (or empathizes when it should educate). You get ads that look different but feel identical to the algorithm and to the viewer.

## MICRO PERSONAS (4 COMPONENTS)

Every script targets a single micro persona built from four layers. Each layer controls a different aspect of the script.

### Component 1: TEEP Mapping (Awareness Level)

Where the viewer sits in their journey determines how much education, proof, and selling you need to do.

**T - Trigger (Problem Aware)**
The viewer is actively experiencing the trigger to purchase. They know something is wrong but have not started looking for solutions yet.

Script implications: Lead with the problem. Name it precisely. Agitate it. Show you understand their daily reality. The product enters late, after trust is built. Heavy on empathy, light on mechanism.

Example: A man who just got winded walking up stairs and realized his fitness has declined. He is not googling supplements yet. He is sitting on the couch feeling disappointed in himself.

**E - Exploration (Solution Aware)**
The viewer knows solutions exist. They may have tried several. Some worked partially. Some were garbage. They are open but skeptical.

Script implications: Acknowledge what they have already tried. Dismiss failed alternatives specifically (not "other products" but "those vitamin D gummies from Amazon"). Introduce the mechanism - the hidden reason previous solutions failed and this one works. Medium education depth.

Example: A woman who has tried three different sleep supplements and still wakes up at 3am. She is ready to try something new but expects to be disappointed.

**E - Evaluation (Product Aware)**
The viewer knows about your product specifically. They have seen an ad, visited the site, maybe added to cart. They are evaluating whether to buy.

Script implications: Do not re-explain the problem. They know. Focus on proof, differentiation, and objection handling. Reviews, clinical data, expert endorsements, guarantees. The script assumes knowledge and provides reasons to act now.

Example: Someone who saw a Surreal ad last week, checked the website, compared macros, but did not buy because they were not sure it would actually taste good.

**P - Purchase (Most Aware)**
The viewer is basically ready to buy. They need a nudge, not a pitch. An offer, a reminder, a piece of social proof that tips the balance.

Script implications: Short. Punchy. Offer-led or proof-led. No education needed. These scripts can be 15-30 seconds. The CTA does the heavy lifting.

Example: Someone who has bought Zapply before and needs to reorder but keeps forgetting.

### Component 2: Core Fears and Desires

This is where most strategists stop too early. They identify a surface-level desire and write to it. But surface desires produce surface scripts.

**The "Why" Chain:** Go beneath the surface by asking "why" repeatedly until you hit a universal human need. The deeper you go, the more emotionally resonant the script becomes.

Example chain for a testosterone supplement:
- Surface: "wants to build muscle" (functional)
- One layer down: "wants to look strong" (appearance)
- Two layers: "wants to feel confident with his shirt off" (self-image)
- Three layers: "wants to turn heads at the beach reunion" (social validation)
- Core: "wants to feel like the man he used to be" (identity)

A script targeting "wants to build muscle" sounds like a product review. A script targeting "wants to feel like the man he used to be" sounds like it was written by someone who knows exactly what he is going through.

**Different micro personas within the same macro audience have different core fears.** Each core fear produces a fundamentally different script.

For the same testosterone supplement:
- Fear 1: "I'm becoming invisible to my wife" - script agitates relationship decline
- Fear 2: "I'm falling behind younger guys at work" - script agitates professional status
- Fear 3: "My body is betraying me" - script agitates physical decline
- Fear 4: "I'm turning into my dad" - script agitates generational fear

These are NOT four ways to phrase the same thing. They are four genuinely different emotional entry points that reach four different segments of the audience.

### Component 3: Cognitive Bias (Psychological Shortcut)

Each persona has a dominant psychological shortcut that determines what TYPE of proof will resonate most. Matching the bias to the proof format is the difference between a script that informs and a script that converts.

**1. Social Proof Bias**
The viewer trusts the crowd. If other people like them have done it, it must be right.

Creative formats: UGC testimonials, review screenshot compilations, "join 50,000+ customers" language, before/after from real customers.

Script markers: Customer quotes, first names, specific results, review counts, community language ("join," "thousands of men like you").

**2. Risk / Loss Aversion Bias**
The viewer is more motivated by what they might lose than what they might gain. Fear of making a wrong choice outweighs excitement about making the right one.

Creative formats: Money-back guarantees front and center, free trial offers, "nothing to lose" framing, honest downside acknowledgment.

Script markers: Guarantee early in the script (not buried at the end), risk reversal as a selling argument, "worst case scenario you get your money back" language.

**3. Authority Bias**
The viewer defers to experts, credentials, and data. They want to be told what to do by someone who knows more than they do.

Creative formats: Doctor/expert spokesperson, podcast-style interviews, clinical study citations, publication endorsements.

Script markers: Credentials mentioned early, specific data points ("clinically shown to increase X by 47%"), third-party validation, institutional language.

**4. Effort Minimization Bias**
The viewer wants the result but fears the effort. They have failed before because the solution was too complicated, too time-consuming, or too disruptive to their routine.

Creative formats: "Takes 30 seconds a day" demonstrations, timeline content showing how easy the routine is, simplicity demos, "just add to your morning coffee."

Script markers: Time anchors ("30 seconds," "one scoop"), routine integration language, "no lifestyle change required," comparisons to things they already do.

**5. Novelty / Pattern Interrupt Bias**
The viewer is desensitized to standard ad formats. They respond to the unexpected - something that breaks the pattern of their feed.

Creative formats: Controversial hooks, unexpected visuals, "what I would NEVER do" framing, meme-style formats, native device mockups.

Script markers: Opening lines that do not sound like ads, visual disruption, contrarian takes, hooks that create curiosity gaps.

**6. Identity Reinforcement Bias**
The viewer buys things that reinforce who they believe they are (or want to become). The product is a badge, not just a tool.

Creative formats: Value-led messaging, community framing, mission-driven content, "for people who..." language.

Script markers: Identity statements ("for men who refuse to settle"), tribe language, shared values, lifestyle alignment.

### Component 4: Creative Consumption

How the target audience actually consumes content determines format, pacing, length, and visual style.

**Reddit-Heavy / Long-Form Consumers**
Longer scripts work. Storytelling-driven. They want depth, evidence, and nuance. They are suspicious of hype and respond to authentic, detailed arguments.

Script implications: 90-120 second scripts. Educational body structures. Mechanism-heavy. Let the proof accumulate gradually. Do not rush the CTA.

**Gen Z TikTok-Native**
Shorter scripts. More visual. Attention-grabbing hooks are non-negotiable. The first 2 seconds determine everything. Quick cuts, dynamic pacing, trend-aware formatting.

Script implications: 30-60 second scripts. Hook must be the strongest element. Visual variety every 3-5 seconds. Conversational, informal language. Avoid anything that feels "produced."

**60+ Facebook Users**
2-3 minute videos work when the content is genuinely useful. Slower pace. Raw, authentic feel. The creator should look and sound like someone in their peer group.

Script implications: Longer scripts with a relaxed pace. Someone who looks like them delivering the message. No rapid cuts. No trendy formatting. Warmth, sincerity, and thoroughness over polish.

**Instagram Discovery Shoppers**
Visual-first. The aesthetic matters as much as the message. They are browsing, not searching. The ad needs to fit into a feed of aspirational content.

Script implications: High production value. Beautiful product shots. Lifestyle integration. The product should feel like a natural extension of an aspirational life, not a solution to a problem.

## SELF-CONCEPT ANCHORS

Three identity layers that produce genuinely different scripts. This is one of the most overlooked dimensions in creative strategy.

### Actual Self
Focuses on the problem as experienced RIGHT NOW. Heavy agitation. "This is your current reality and it is not okay."

Script voice: Second person, present tense. "You wake up tired. You drag yourself to work. You come home and collapse on the couch."

The power: Immediacy. The viewer sees their own life reflected back and feels the urgency to change.

Risk: Can feel depressing if agitation is too heavy without a clear path to resolution.

### Ideal Self
Focuses on the after-state. Aspirational and transformational. "This is who you could become."

Script voice: Future tense or testimonial past tense. "Imagine waking up with energy" or "Three months ago I could barely climb stairs. Now I run 5Ks."

The power: Hope. The viewer sees a version of themselves they want to become and associates that transformation with the product.

Risk: Can feel like empty promises if not grounded in specific, believable proof.

### Ought Self
Third-person perspective. "My partner is suffering." "My kids deserve better." Motivated by duty, guilt, responsibility, or care for someone else.

Script voice: Third person or relationship-framed. "When you watch your mum struggle with..." or "Your kids trust you to..."

The power: Guilt and duty are powerful motivators that bypass the "I do not deserve nice things" barrier many people have when buying for themselves.

Risk: Can feel manipulative if the guilt is too heavy-handed. The care must feel genuine.

### Why This Matters for Hook Diversity
Two hooks that both address the viewer's current problem ("You feel tired all the time" and "Waking up exhausted every day") are NOT testing different self-concept anchors. They are both Actual Self hooks with different phrasing.

True self-concept testing:
- Hook 1 (Actual Self): "You used to have energy. What happened?"
- Hook 2 (Ideal Self): "What if you could feel 25 again?"
- Hook 3 (Ought Self): "Your kids notice when you're too tired to play."

Each of these reaches a psychologically different version of the same person.

## VALENCE ZONES (EMOTIONAL CHARGE)

Four quadrants that map the emotional territory of your script. Most ad accounts accidentally cluster in one zone, leaving three others completely untapped.

### Zone 1: Positive, Low Arousal
Calm, cozy, supportive, nurturing. "Everything is going to be okay."

Script tone: Warm, gentle, reassuring. Think: a friend who cares about you telling you about something that helped them.

Works for: Products associated with comfort, routine, self-care. Audiences who are burned out on aggressive marketing.

Example: "I used to stress about breakfast every morning. Now I just pour a bowl and my whole morning feels calmer."

### Zone 2: Positive, High Arousal
Excitement, joy, empowerment, confidence. "You are about to change your life."

Script tone: Energetic, inspiring, momentum-building. Think: a coach who genuinely believes in you.

Works for: Fitness products, transformation stories, aspirational brands. Audiences who are motivated by possibility.

Example: "Imagine walking into that reunion and every single person asking what you have been doing differently."

### Zone 3: Negative, Low Arousal
Annoyance, frustration, disappointment, resignation. "This is getting old."

Script tone: Tired, relatable, mildly frustrated. Think: someone who has been putting up with something for too long and is finally ready to do something about it.

Works for: Products that solve chronic, low-grade problems. Audiences in the Exploration phase who have tried things that did not work.

Example: "You have tried the supplements. You have tried the diet changes. You have tried sleeping earlier. And you still wake up exhausted."

### Zone 4: Negative, High Arousal
Panic, fear, loss, anxiety, urgency. "If you do not act, this will get worse."

Script tone: Urgent, alarming, high-stakes. Think: a doctor telling you results you were not expecting.

Works for: Health products addressing serious conditions. Audiences in the Trigger phase experiencing acute symptoms.

Example: "At 8:30am they get a massive blood sugar spike. By 9:30, they crash. By 10:30, they are starving. And this happens every single day."

### The Clustering Problem
Most ad accounts live in Zone 4. Every script agitates fear. Every hook creates anxiety. Every body escalates urgency.

This works - until it stops working. The audience gets desensitized to fear-based messaging. Or worse, the algorithm clusters all your ads against the same narrow audience segment that responds to fear.

Writing in Zone 1 or 2 opens audiences that fear-based scripts never reach. The person who scrolls past a panic-inducing health ad might stop for a calm, warm testimonial about how a product made their mornings better.

Diversify across all four zones. Track which zone each script sits in and deliberately fill gaps.

## THE RULE OF ONE

One avatar. One desire. One message.

If an ad mentions brain fog, back pain, AND menopause, someone who only has menopause scrolls past because the ad was not talking to them - it was talking to everyone and therefore no one.

The narrower the persona, the wider the resonance. An ad targeting "diabetic women over 50 who miss eating cereal for breakfast" (extremely narrow) produced T1 performance (SUR3) because every word of that script felt personally written for the viewer.

Broad targeting in the ad platform is fine. Narrow targeting in the creative is mandatory.

## HOOK DIVERSITY PRINCIPLE

True hook diversity means each hook taps a DIFFERENT psychological layer, not the same layer with different words.

### What Real Diversity Looks Like
- Hook 1: Agitates a core desire (Ideal Self, Zone 2)
- Hook 2: Agitates a core fear (Actual Self, Zone 4)
- Hook 3: Utilizes a key cognitive bias (Authority, Zone 3)
- Hook 4: Pattern interrupt through unexpected format (Novelty, Zone 1)

### What Fake Diversity Looks Like
- Hook 1: "Tired of feeling exhausted every morning?"
- Hook 2: "What if you could finally wake up with energy?"
- Hook 3: "Most people don't realize their morning fatigue is caused by..."

These all target the same desire (energy), the same self-concept (actual self), and the same valence zone (Zone 3/4). Different words, same psychological address. The algorithm treats them as essentially the same ad.

### The Diversity Audit
Before finalizing hook variations, tag each one:
1. Which self-concept anchor? (Actual / Ideal / Ought)
2. Which valence zone? (1 / 2 / 3 / 4)
3. Which cognitive bias? (Social proof / Authority / Risk aversion / Effort min / Novelty / Identity)

If any two hooks share the same tags across all three dimensions, one of them needs to be rewritten from a different psychological angle.

## THREE DIMENSIONS OF PSYCHOLOGICALLY DISTINCT ADS

Tag every script across these three dimensions to ensure genuine creative diversity across a batch.

### Dimension 1: Valence Zone
Which emotional quadrant does this script primarily live in?
- Zone 1 (positive, low arousal)
- Zone 2 (positive, high arousal)
- Zone 3 (negative, low arousal)
- Zone 4 (negative, high arousal)

### Dimension 2: Self-Concept Anchor
Whose perspective drives the narrative?
- Actual self (present problem)
- Ideal self (future transformation)
- Ought self (duty to others)

### Dimension 3: Language Intensity
How "hard" is the sell?
- Low intensity: Educational, informational, top-of-funnel. The script teaches. The viewer learns something and trusts the brand.
- Medium intensity: Balanced. Problem-solution with proof. The viewer feels understood and sees a path forward.
- High intensity: Direct response, bottom-of-funnel. The script pushes for action. Urgency, scarcity, strong CTA.

### Using the Tags
A batch of 5 scripts should cover at least 3 valence zones, at least 2 self-concept anchors, and a range of language intensities.

If all 5 scripts are Zone 4 / Actual Self / High Intensity, the batch will cannibalize itself. The ads compete for the same narrow psychological audience.

## PERSONA-TO-SCRIPT MAPPING

This table shows exactly how each persona element translates into a specific script section.

| Persona Element | Script Section It Controls | How It Shows Up |
|----------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| Core motivator (desire) | Hook | The desire is what stops the scroll. "What if you could..." or the aspiration that creates the gap. |
| Core fear | Problem agitation | The fear creates urgency. "If you don't fix this, here's what happens..." |
| Cognitive bias | Proof type | The bias determines what KIND of proof lands. Authority bias = expert quotes. Social proof bias = customer reviews. |
| Awareness level (TEEP) | Mechanism depth | Trigger = no mechanism, pure empathy. Exploration = full mechanism education. Evaluation = compressed mechanism, heavy proof. Purchase = no mechanism, offer-led. |
| Self-concept anchor | Voice and POV | Actual self = second person present ("you feel..."). Ideal self = future/testimonial ("imagine..." or "I used to..."). Ought self = third person ("your kids..."). |
| Creative consumption | Format and pacing | Reddit-heavy = 90-120s, educational. TikTok-native = 30-60s, fast cuts. Facebook 60+ = 2-3min, slow and warm. |

## ANDROMEDA AND CREATIVE DIVERSITY

Meta's Andromeda ranking system analyzes "visual signatures" of ads. It does not just read copy - it processes visual patterns, creator faces, settings, color palettes, and camera angles.

### What This Means for Creative Strategy
Same creator + same setting + different script = NOT enough differentiation for Andromeda. The system sees them as variations of the same ad and serves them to overlapping audiences.

### What True Differentiation Requires
- Different talent (different faces, different body types, different ages)
- Different backgrounds (bedroom vs kitchen vs outdoors vs office)
- Different camera angles (talking head vs product demo vs screen recording)
- Different messaging approaches (education vs testimonial vs comparison)
- Different visual treatments (raw UGC vs polished vs graphic overlay)

### The 25 vs 200 Rule
25 genuinely different creative variants beat 200 variations of the same thing. Every time.

200 variations of the same creator in the same room with slightly different scripts gives Andromeda 200 versions of one ad to serve to one audience.

25 variants with different talent, different settings, different messaging angles, and different visual treatments gives Andromeda 25 genuinely distinct assets to match against 25 different audience segments.

Spend creative energy on differentiation, not variation.

## THE COMPOUNDING RESEARCH LOOP

Research > Personas > Angles > Scripts > Testing > Learnings > Research

This is not a one-time process. It is a loop that compounds over time.

### How It Works in Practice

**Round 1:** Research the brand, product, audience, reviews, Reddit threads, competitor ads. Build initial persona set. Write scripts. Launch.

**Round 2:** Performance data comes back. One hook dramatically outperformed. Why? It targeted a fear that was not in the original research. Go deeper on that fear. Build new personas around it. Write new scripts. Launch.

**Round 3:** The new fear-based scripts outperform across multiple creatives. The audience segment is now understood better. Research adjacent fears. Test new self-concept anchors against the same fear. Write new scripts. Launch.

Each round makes the persona model more accurate. After 3-4 rounds, the strategist has a map of the audience's psychology that no amount of initial research could produce.

### What to Track Between Rounds
- Which hooks won and what psychological layer they tapped
- Which body structures held attention (mechanism vs empathy vs social proof)
- Which CTAs converted (guarantee-led vs urgency vs soft)
- Which creator types resonated (expert vs peer vs aspirational)
- Which awareness levels responded (are we reaching Trigger or just Evaluation?)

Feed these learnings back into the persona model. Update the micro personas. Write the next batch from a place of knowledge, not guesswork.

## COMMON PERSONA MISTAKES

### 1. Demographic Personas
"Women 25-45 interested in skincare" is not a persona. It is a media buying audience. A persona is: "A 34-year-old woman who has tried Curology and The Ordinary, still breaks out before her period, and is embarrassed to go to work without concealer because her manager once commented on her skin."

### 2. One Persona Per Brand
Most brands need 4-8 micro personas to cover their addressable audience. A testosterone supplement buyer who is motivated by bedroom performance is a completely different person (psychologically) from one motivated by gym performance, even though they are buying the same product.

### 3. Static Personas
Personas evolve as you learn. The persona set for Batch 1 should look different from the persona set for Batch 4. Each batch teaches you something about who actually responds.

### 4. Persona Without Proof Matching
Building a beautiful persona and then using the wrong proof type. If the persona's dominant bias is social proof but the script leads with clinical data, the proof does not land. Match the bias to the proof.
