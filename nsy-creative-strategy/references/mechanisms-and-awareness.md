# Mechanisms, Awareness Stages, and the Two-Promise Architecture

**Purpose:** How to build the mechanism that makes an ad persuasive, how to calibrate scripts to the viewer's awareness level, and how to separate the two types of promises in every script. This is the strategic foundation underneath the structures in structures-and-hooks.md.

---

## THE TWO-PART MECHANISM: UMP AND UMS

Every high-performing health/wellness/beauty ad answers two questions the viewer did not know they had:

1. **UMP (Unique Mechanism of the Problem):** The real, surprising reason the prospect has been failing. This fills a knowledge gap. It reframes their past failures as "not your fault" by naming a hidden cause.

2. **UMS (Unique Mechanism of the Solution):** How to fix the newly revealed problem. This must follow directly and logically from the UMP. If the UMP says "your calves are too weak to pump blood back up," the UMS must address calf strength, not something unrelated.

### The Logical Chain

Every mechanism-driven ad follows this chain, whether it takes 15 seconds or 2 minutes:

**Problem** (the pain the viewer already knows)
> **Real Cause / UMP** (the surprising reason behind the pain)
> **Specific Solution / UMS** (how to fix the newly revealed cause)
> **Product delivers that solution** (the product is the vehicle, not the star)

When this chain breaks - when the UMS does not logically follow from the UMP, or the product does not clearly deliver the UMS - the ad feels like a bait-and-switch.

---

## THE THREE-PART UMP PATTERN (for health/wellness/beauty)

The strongest UMPs in this space follow three beats:

### Beat 1: How the System is SUPPOSED to Work
Establish the baseline. The viewer needs to understand the healthy version before they can understand the breakdown. Keep this to one sentence.

### Beat 2: Why the System is NOT Working
The "missing 1%" - the specific point of failure. This is the insight that makes the viewer think "I never knew that." It should feel like a discovery, not a lecture.

### Beat 3: What External Factors Cause the Malfunction
Make it personal and believable by connecting the malfunction to something in the viewer's life. Age, diet, environment, stress, medication side effects. This is what makes the viewer think "that's ME."

### Real Examples of This Pattern

**Nooro (leg circulation):**
1. Your heart pumps blood down to your legs
2. Your calves are supposed to pump it back up (they are your "second heart")
3. But sitting all day weakens the calf muscles, so blood pools in your lower legs

**Beverly Hills MD (wrinkles):**
1. Collagen is what keeps your skin firm and elastic
2. DHT (a hormone byproduct) actively inhibits collagen production
3. Menopause causes DHT to spike, which is why wrinkles seem to appear overnight after 45

**Emma Relief (bloating/constipation):**
1. Your digestive system is supposed to move food through in 24-36 hours
2. Certain gut bacteria produce excess methane gas
3. That methane damages the nerves that control gut movement, creating a cycle that gets worse over time

In each case: baseline > breakdown > personal trigger. Three beats. The viewer understands the problem in a new way.

## THE SIMPLICITY TEST

If you cannot hear a mechanism once and explain it to someone else in 30 seconds, it is too complicated.

For 30-60 second ads, the mechanism must compress to 2-3 sentences without losing its logic. If it takes a paragraph to explain, go back and simplify until the core insight fits in two sentences with one analogy.

This test applies at the concepting stage, not during script writing. If the mechanism fails this test, no amount of good writing will fix it.

## MECHANISM NAMING PRINCIPLES

The mechanism often gets a name. That name matters more than you think.

**Good mechanism names:**
- Intuitive grasp (the name hints at the concept without explanation)
- Sound real ("Dermal-Epidermal Junction" is credible because it uses real anatomical terms)
- Easy to remember (1-3 words max)
- Not a fancy label for something obvious

**Bad mechanism names:**
- Made-up science words that sound fake ("Bio-Tensegrity System")
- Names that require explanation to make sense
- Longer than 3 words
- Names that rename a concept the viewer already knows (calling fiber "Digestive Sweep Technology" insults intelligence)

The best mechanism names make the viewer feel like they learned something real. The worst ones make the viewer feel like they are being marketed to.

---

## ANALOGY IS THE COMPRESSION TOOL

When you cannot explain the full science in 15 seconds, one analogy does the work:

- "Like adding more cars to a traffic jam" (why extra fiber makes constipation worse)
- "Those crystals are like tiny needles stabbing inside your joints" (uric acid and gout)
- "Your calves are basically a second heart" (Nooro leg circulation)
- "It's like trying to build a house with no foundation" (collagen and aging skin)

**One good analogy replaces an entire mechanism explanation.**

This is the single most important compression tool in the kit. At 30 seconds, the analogy IS the mechanism section. At 90 seconds, the analogy opens the mechanism section and the science backs it up.

Develop the analogy during concepting, not during script writing. If you are writing the script and the mechanism section feels too long, you do not have a good enough analogy yet.

---

## WHEN TO SKIP THE MECHANISM

Not every ad needs a mechanism. Skip it when:

- **UGC where authenticity matters more than education.** The creator's personal story and transformation IS the argument. Adding a mechanism makes it feel scripted.
- **Social proof heavy ads.** "10,000 women solved this problem" IS the mechanism. The volume of proof replaces the need to explain why it works.
- **Most Aware audiences.** They already know how it works. Offer, urgency, and social proof matter more than education. Do not re-educate someone who is ready to buy.

When you skip the mechanism, the script leans on Structure 2 (Personal Discovery) or a testimonial compilation. The proof carries the weight that the mechanism normally would.

## THE LUCKY STRIKE PRINCIPLE

In a saturated market, the first brand to name a mechanism wins - even if the mechanism is universally true.

Lucky Strike cigarettes ran the headline "It's Toasted." Every cigarette was toasted. That was how all cigarettes were made. But Lucky Strike was the first to say it out loud, and it became THEIR claim.

For DTC health/wellness brands: most supplements use similar ingredients. Most skincare uses similar actives. The brand that names the mechanism first - that puts a label on the biological process and explains it simply - owns that mechanism in the viewer's mind.

This is why mechanism development matters so much at the strategy stage. You are not just explaining science. You are claiming territory.

## MECHANISM IDEATION PROCESS

How NSY develops mechanisms for new brands:

1. **Run ideation across multiple LLMs** with a unified research document (competitor ads, ingredient studies, customer reviews, brand claims)
2. **Specify the awareness level** of the target audience - this changes what kind of mechanism works
3. **Cherry-pick promising mechanisms** from the outputs
4. **Give feedback and iterate** - push for simpler analogies, more surprising angles, better names
5. **Evaluate using triangulation** - run the top 3-5 mechanisms across 3 different LLMs, compare how they rank them, look for consensus on the strongest

### Mechanism Evaluation Criteria

**Ranks high when:**
- Speaks to the correct awareness level
- Diagnoses emotional pain (not just physical symptoms)
- Reframes past failures and removes self-blame ("It's not your fault, it's because...")
- Passes the simplicity test (2-3 sentences, one analogy)
- Differentiated from what competitors are saying

**Ranks low when:**
- Assumes wrong knowledge level (too basic for solution-aware, too complex for problem-aware)
- Fancy name for something obvious
- Too complex to explain in 30 seconds
- Focuses on solution delivery instead of problem diagnosis (UMS without UMP)
- Uses the same angle every competitor uses

## AWARENESS STAGES

The viewer's awareness level changes everything about the script. Same product, same mechanism, completely different ad depending on where the viewer sits on this spectrum.

### Unaware
They do not know they have a problem. You have to start from scratch.

**Hook strategy:** Ethical fear or belief challenge. "What if the breakfast you give your kid every morning is dissolving their brainpower?" You are creating awareness of a problem they did not know existed.

**Script approach:** Longer education. More time on problem establishment. The mechanism has to do heavy lifting because the viewer has no frame of reference.

### Problem Aware
They know the problem. They feel the pain. They do not know solutions exist (or have not seriously looked).

**Hook strategy:** Raw frustration. Symptom stacks. "If you're over 40 and you feel weaker than you ever have..." Speak to the pain they are already experiencing.

**Script approach:** UMP names WHY they are suffering. This is the "aha moment" that shifts them from "I have a problem" to "now I understand WHY." Most common target for broad markets.

### Solution Aware
They know solutions exist. They have probably tried some. They have not found the right one.

**Hook strategy:** Failed solutions. "Nothing works because..." Address the frustration of having tried and failed.

**Script approach:** Differentiate with mechanism. Explain why everything else fails (using the UMP) and why this approach is different (using the UMS). Sweet spot for supplements, skincare, and most DTC health/wellness.

### Product Aware
They know specific products. They may know yours. They are comparing.

**Hook strategy:** Core objections. Address the specific hesitation keeping them from buying. Price, skepticism, ingredient concerns, "does it actually work?"

**Script approach:** Mechanism must be more sophisticated because the viewer has a higher baseline of knowledge. Address specific hesitations directly. Heavy proof section.

### Most Aware
They know your product. They have visited the page. They might have items in cart.

**Hook strategy:** Offer-led. "Last chance for 40% off." Social proof at scale. "Join 50,000+ customers."

**Script approach:** Minimal education. Offer, urgency, social proof. Do not re-educate them. Close the sale.

---

## HOW AWARENESS CHANGES SCRIPT ELEMENTS

| Element | Problem Aware | Solution Aware | Product Aware |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | Raw frustration, symptom stack | Failed solutions, "nothing works because..." | Core objections, skepticism |
| **Mechanism depth** | Light (name + analogy) | Medium (why alternatives fail + your mechanism) | Deep (technical, higher baseline assumed) |
| **Proof type** | Volume claims, authority ("doctors recommend") | Specific results, comparison to alternatives | Heavy data, before/after, clinical studies |
| **CTA style** | Educational ("learn more", "see how it works") | Solution-focused ("try this", "see if it works for you") | Offer-led ("get 40% off", "free shipping today") |

The same product can have three completely different scripts for three awareness levels. A supplement brand might run:
- A problem-aware ad on broad prospecting (symptom stack hook, light mechanism, authority proof)
- A solution-aware ad on interest-based targeting (failed solutions hook, full mechanism, comparison proof)
- A product-aware ad on retargeting (objection hook, minimal mechanism, offer-led CTA)

## THE TWO-PROMISE ARCHITECTURE

This is the most important structural insight for writing scripts that convert. Every script contains two types of promises, and they must be kept separate.

### Teaser Promises (sell the VIDEO)

These make the viewer want to keep watching. They become the HOOK and the re-hook moments throughout the script.

**Examples:**
- "There's a hidden reason your eye cream doesn't work"
- "What your doctor isn't telling you about testosterone after 40"
- "I found something that changed my sleep in 3 days"

Teaser promises create curiosity. They open loops. They do NOT mention the product by name and they do NOT make specific claims about results.

### Bold Product Claims (sell the PRODUCT)

These make the viewer want to buy. They become the PROOF section and the CTA.

**Examples:**
- "39% wrinkle reduction in 4 weeks"
- "Clinically shown to increase deep sleep by 45 minutes"
- "Over 10,000 5-star reviews"
- "90-day money-back guarantee"

Bold product claims close loops. They deliver specifics. They name the product, cite data, and make the case for purchase.

### Why This Split Matters

Without this separation, hooks try to sell the product (too early, viewer is not ready) and proof sections sound like teasers (too vague, viewer needs specifics to convert).

**The most common AI script failure:** Putting product claims in the hook and teaser language in the proof section. The hook says "Brand X reduces wrinkles by 39%" (that is a proof point, not a hook). The proof section says "imagine waking up with younger-looking skin" (that is a teaser, not proof). Everything ends up sounding the same and nothing does its job.

**The fix:** Teaser promises go in Scenes 1-3 (hook, problem, failed alternative). Bold product claims go in Scenes 4-6 (solution, proof, CTA). The transition point is the product introduction.

## BRIEF-TO-SCRIPT MAPPING

How brief fields map to script sections. This is the bridge between strategy (the brief) and execution (the script).

| Brief Field | Script Section |
|---|---|
| Pain Points (#1) + Teaser Promises | Hook |
| Pain Points (#2, #3) + Existing Solutions | Problem/Agitation |
| UMP + UMS + Analogy | Solution Introduction |
| Ingredient Studies + Product Claims + Future Pacing | Proof/Demo |
| Product Promise + Guarantee | CTA |

When writing a script from a brief:
1. Start with the hook - pull from Pain Point #1 and the strongest teaser promise
2. Build problem agitation from remaining pain points and the "existing solutions that fail" section
3. Deliver the mechanism using the UMP, UMS, and analogy from the brief
4. Stack proof using ingredient studies and bold product claims
5. Close with the product promise and guarantee/offer details

If any of these brief fields are empty or weak, flag it before writing. A script without a clear UMP will default to generic feature-listing. A script without bold product claims will have a weak proof section.

## PROOF ELEMENTS: HOW MUCH SCIENCE AND WHERE

The amount of scientific detail changes based on WHERE in the script you are.

### In the Mechanism Section: Keep It Light

One or two studies maximum. The mechanism section is carried by logic and analogy, not citations. Think of it as a smart friend explaining something surprising over coffee. They might mention "there was a study that showed..." but they are not reading a journal article to you.

**Good:** "Researchers found that methane gas actually paralyzes the nerves in your gut. That's why adding more fiber - which feeds the bacteria producing the methane - can make things worse."

**Bad:** "A 2019 study published in the Journal of Gastroenterology (Smith et al.) demonstrated that methanogenic archaea produce methane concentrations exceeding 10ppm, which correlates with a 47% reduction in intestinal motility as measured by..."

### In the Proof/Product Section: This is Where Data Matters

Specific numbers. Named studies. Before-and-after timelines. This is the section where the viewer has already bought into the mechanism and now needs evidence that THIS product delivers.

**Good:** "In a clinical trial, women saw 39% wrinkle reduction in just 4 weeks." "Over 10,000 verified 5-star reviews." "Dr. Sarah Chen recommends it to her patients."

### The Rule of Thumb

**Mechanism section = smart friend explaining something surprising.**
**Proof section = doctor presenting evidence.**

If you have the tone backwards - heavy citations in the mechanism, vague language in the proof - flip them.

## PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER: THE STRATEGY CHECKLIST

Before writing any script, confirm these five decisions:

1. **Awareness level:** Problem-aware, solution-aware, or product-aware? This sets hook style, mechanism depth, and CTA approach.

2. **Structure:** Which of the 4 NSY structures fits this creator, this awareness level, and this proof library?

3. **Mechanism:** What is the UMP (why they are failing), the UMS (how to fix it), and the analogy (one sentence that replaces a paragraph of science)?

4. **Two promises:** What are the teaser promises (sell the video) and the bold product claims (sell the product)? Are they in the right sections?

5. **Destination:** In-feed ad going to product page (name product, sell directly) or in-feed ad going to VSL/advertorial (tease, do not name product)?

If any of these five are unclear, resolve them before writing. A script written without these decisions made will always feel generic.
