**Purpose:** The primary training reference. These are real NSY scripts that performed at the highest level. Study them before writing anything. The patterns in these scripts ARE the standard.

**Source:** 27 hand-picked winning scripts across 9 brands (Hume Health, Zapply, Surreal, Organised, Gyal Braids, Ankhway, Healthy Metal, Icy Bear, PETL). Performance-tiered by Nativ. Full annotated breakdowns of 3 T1 scripts below.

## HOW TO USE THIS FILE

Do NOT memorize these scripts. Absorb the patterns. When the strategist gives you a brief, your job is to produce a script that FEELS like these examples - same structural logic, same voice, same rhythm - but is completely original for the client's product and audience.

The patterns that matter most:
1. How the hook creates a gap the viewer needs to close
2. How the body educates before it sells
3. How the product enters as a friend's recommendation, not an ad
4. How proof is stacked through specifics, not vague claims
5. How the CTA includes risk reversal as a core selling argument

## WHAT THE TIERS REVEAL

Five patterns emerge when you study what separates T1 from T2 and T3.

**1. Health/medical + educational angles + mechanism-heavy = highest performance.**
Hume Health dominates T1 and T2. When you teach someone something genuinely new about their health, they pay attention. The education IS the hook. The mechanism IS the selling argument. Products in the health space have an unfair advantage here - use it.

**2. "This is what would happen" format replicates proven 9-figure structure.**
ORG3 follows the exact framework that built Ryze into a 9-figure brand. The format works because the hypothetical framing ("what would happen if...") creates curiosity without making a direct claim. The body then delivers research-backed benefits that feel educational rather than promotional.

**3. Founder-led + celebrity proof = T1 level.**
ORG1 (founder story + Bear Grylls) and ORG4 (Sophie Habboo pregnancy) both hit T1. The combination of authentic founder narrative plus celebrity endorsement creates a double credibility stack that is very hard to beat.

**4. Long-form VSL crushes when problem education holds attention.**
ZAP4 at ~90 seconds and SUR1 at ~2 minutes are both T1. Length is not the enemy. Boring is the enemy. When every sentence earns the next sentence, long-form outperforms short-form because it builds a more complete argument.

**5. Empathy-first (validate the struggle before pitching) = T1.**
SUR3 has zero mechanism. No science. No clinical data. Just deep empathy for what it is like to be diabetic and miss eating cereal. That empathy earned permission to sell. Validation before pitch is a T1 pattern.

### Beat-by-Beat Annotation

| Beat | Time | What it does | Why it works |
|---|---|---|---|
| Celebrity hook | 0-8s | Borrowed attention via Bezos + Dana White | Names everyone recognizes. Scroll-stopper. |
| Reframe | 8-15s | "You don't need to inject synthetic testosterone" | Dismisses the extreme solution, opens the door to something accessible |

### Key Patterns to Absorb

1. **Celebrity hook does NOT sell the product.** It sells the next 5 seconds. The celebrities are never mentioned again.
2. **Mechanism is ONE sentence.** "Your body's just lacking the raw materials." That's the entire UMP. Compressed to its essence.
3. **Dismiss alternatives lives inside the symptom stack**, not as a separate section. "tried every supplement on Amazon" is both a mirror (I see you) and a dismiss (those don't work).
4. **Product enters at the 50% mark.** Half the script is pre-product education and identification.
5. **The guarantee is positioned as proof**, not fine print. "Because so many men have been scammed" frames the guarantee as the brand knowing what the audience has been through.
6. **Hook variations change the celebrity, not the structure.** Body script is identical. Only the first 5-8 seconds change. Most efficient testing method.

## THE GAP BETWEEN THEORY AND NSY PRACTICE

When you read script frameworks and advertising theory, certain things are presented as essential. In practice, NSY's winning scripts deviate from textbook advice in specific, consistent ways. These deviations are not mistakes - they are what makes the scripts work.

### No Formal "Big Idea" Statement
Advertising theory says every script needs a clear, articulated Big Idea. NSY's winning scripts have a Big Idea, but it is never stated explicitly. It is FELT through the narrative.

ZAP4's Big Idea is "your body already has the machinery, it just needs the raw materials." But no line in the script says that as a headline-style statement. The mechanism sentence ("Your body's just lacking the raw materials to produce it") expresses it conversationally. The Big Idea is implicit, woven into the texture of the argument.

When writing, do not insert a Big Idea line. Let the mechanism and narrative carry the concept.

### Product Introduction is Gradual, Not a Reveal
Theory frameworks often have a "reveal moment" where the product enters with fanfare. NSY scripts do the opposite. The product slides in like a friend's recommendation.

"All those ingredients and more are in Zapply" - not "Introducing Zapply!" The product is positioned as the logical conclusion of the education, not a surprise.

"That's exactly why I created Surreal" - the product enters as the founder's personal response to the problem, not as a commercial pivot.

The product should feel like it was already part of the conversation, not like the conversation was building to a commercial.

### CTAs are Softer Than Theory Recommends
Most DR advertising theory pushes hard CTAs: "Buy now!" "Don't miss out!" "Act fast!" NSY's winning CTAs are consistently softer than that.

"Tap below while it's still in stock" - soft urgency, not desperation.
"Give it a try" - invitation, not command.
"Try Surreal, and take back control of your mornings" - the emotional benefit IS the CTA.

The guarantee does the heavy lifting that a hard CTA would normally do. When the risk is removed, the viewer does not need to be pushed. They need permission.

### Voice and Dialect Are Strategic Conversion Assets
Creator accent, dialect, and speaking style are not incidental. They are targeting mechanisms. A Scottish creator talking about testosterone supplements signals something different than a London creator saying the same words.

When briefing creators, the voice is as strategic as the script. Match the creator's natural speaking style to the audience segment you are targeting.

### Emotional Testimonials Beat Mechanism in Some Categories
For Surreal (cereal), emotional testimonials from diabetics (SUR3) outperformed mechanism-heavy scripts. For Hume Health (supplements), mechanism-heavy scripts outperformed testimonials.

The lesson: the category determines which persuasion mode wins. Health supplements with complex mechanisms benefit from education. Food products with emotional connections benefit from empathy and social proof.

Do not default to mechanism for every category. Read the category first.

## 4 BODY STRUCTURE ARCHETYPES

### A: Problem Timeline Cascade (60-120s)
Scripts: SUR1, SUR3, ZAP4, ZAP12, HH24

The signature NSY move. Walk the viewer through what happens step by step. Timestamps, symptoms, escalation. Creates an unavoidable vision of either the problem worsening or the solution working.

When to use: When the problem has a clear temporal progression. When specificity drives urgency. When the viewer needs to SEE the problem playing out in their own life.

### B: Conversational UGC Storytelling (45-75s)
Scripts: ZAP1, ZAP6, ANK02, GB37

The creator IS the audience. Their personal story is the proof. No mechanism needed when the transformation is compelling enough. The structure follows a natural storytelling arc: "I had this problem, I tried everything, then I found this, and it changed everything."

When to use: When you have a creator who genuinely matches the target audience. When the transformation story is specific and believable. When the category is more emotional than scientific.

### C: Educational Authority (45-90s)
Scripts: HH15, HH17, HH24, HH33, HH52, HM6

Expert or pseudo-expert teaches something genuinely valuable. The education earns trust. The product is positioned as the tool that makes the education actionable. "Now that you understand WHY this happens, here is how to fix it."

When to use: When there is a genuine mechanism to explain. When the audience values knowledge. When competitor products cannot match the science. When the product has clinical backing or ingredient differentiation.

### D: Quick Hit / Format Piece (30-45s)
Scripts: HH28, HH29, ORG3, ORG7, PTL9

Rapid information delivery. Visual-heavy. The hook does most of the work. The body is proof-stacking with minimal narration. These scripts rely on visual storytelling and tight editing rather than long-form copy.

When to use: When the hook IS the argument. When the visual assets are strong. When testing a new angle quickly before committing to long-form.

## PRODUCT INTRODUCTION PATTERNS

The product never enters with a hard pivot. It flows naturally from the education or story that preceded it.

**"But it doesn't have to be that way" bridge** (SUR1, SUR3)
The empathy bridge. After fully validating the problem, this phrase signals that hope is coming. The product enters as the answer to a question the viewer is already asking.

**"Here's what I take/recommend" personal endorsement** (ANK02, ZAP1, ZAP6)
The creator shares what worked for them. The product enters as a personal recommendation, not a pitch. Works when the creator has genuine credibility with the audience.

**"That's why I started recommending" professional endorsement** (HH24)
The expert has been teaching for 30-45 seconds. The product enters as the expert's professional recommendation. The education earned the authority to recommend.

**"What if you could get the same thing" comparison pivot** (HH51, HH52)
Connects the product to something aspirational or expensive. "What if you could get the same ingredients that elite athletes use, but in one capsule?" The product enters as the accessible version of something desirable.

**Direct product education woven in** (GB37, ZAP4)
No transition moment. The product information is threaded throughout the script so naturally that there is no "and now, the product" moment. The viewer absorbs product knowledge without a commercial break.

## CTA PATTERNS

Nearly every winning CTA includes risk reversal. The guarantee is not an afterthought. It is a core selling argument.

**Guarantee-led (most common):** "Feel the difference, or you pay nothing" / "Try it risk-free for 30 days. Every penny back." The guarantee removes the last objection and reframes the purchase as a no-risk trial.

**Urgency/scarcity:** "Tap below while it's still in stock" / "Don't wait another month getting weaker." Works when paired with genuine stock limitations or time-bound offers.

**Soft/conversational:** "Give it a try" / "Try Surreal, and take back control of your mornings." Works in empathy-driven scripts where a hard CTA would break the tone.

**Value stack with price anchoring:** "The same stack costs 250 buying separately. You get it for a fraction." The CTA completes a value argument that started in the body.

## NSY SCRIPT FRAMEWORKS

Six proven frameworks from the NSY Wiki. Each one serves a different strategic purpose.

### PAS (Problem - Agitate - Solution)
The classic. Name the problem, make it worse, present the product as the fix. Use when the problem is well-understood by the audience and agitation drives urgency. Most effective for Problem Aware audiences.

### AIDA (Attention - Interest - Desire - Action)
Grab attention, build interest through education, create desire through proof/benefits, drive action with CTA. Use when you need a complete persuasion arc from cold attention to purchase intent. Works across awareness levels.

### Enemy Framework
Position a common enemy (Big Pharma, processed food industry, misleading marketing) and the product as the antidote. Use when the audience already distrusts the status quo. Creates an "us vs them" dynamic that bonds the viewer to the brand.

### QUEST (Qualify - Understand - Educate - Stimulate - Transition)
Open with a qualifier ("if you're over 40..."), demonstrate understanding, educate on the mechanism, stimulate desire through proof, transition to CTA. Use for longer scripts targeting specific demographics. The qualifier in the first line filters the audience.

### Outcome Framework
Lead with the result. "What would your life look like if..." Open with the transformation, then work backwards to explain how the product delivers it. Use when the outcome is highly desirable and the audience is Solution Aware or higher.

### FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
Others are already benefiting. The product is selling fast. The offer is limited. Use sparingly and only when the urgency is genuine. Best for retargeting and Most Aware audiences who need a push.

## CONCEPT FORMAT LIBRARY (25+ FORMATS)

These are the creative formats available in the NSY Wiki. Each one is a distinct visual and narrative approach.

### Talking Head Formats
- **Credible Narrator** - Expert or pseudo-expert delivers information to camera
- **Man on the Street** - Vox-pop style interviews with real people
- **Founder Story** - Brand founder tells their origin story and mission
- **Direct Confession** - Creator shares a vulnerable personal truth

### Visual-Led Formats
- **Split Screen** - Side-by-side comparison (with/without, before/after)
- **ASMR** - Sensory-focused product demonstration with enhanced audio
- **Unboxing** - First impression of product arrival
- **Product Demo** - How the product works in real-time
- **Routine Integration** - Product shown within daily routine context

### Platform-Native Formats
- **TikTok Response** - "Replying to @user" format that feels like organic content
- **Sticky Note / Text Overlay** - Text-driven with minimal production
- **Green Screen** - Creator reacts to content on screen behind them
- **Duet / Stitch Style** - Reacting to or building on another video
- **Apple Notes** - Disguised as a device screenshot

### Story-Driven Formats
- **Podcast Style** - Two people discussing the product naturally
- **Story Time** - "Let me tell you what happened..." narrative
- **Day in the Life** - Product woven into a full day's activities
- **Get Ready With Me (GRWM)** - Product featured during a getting-ready routine

### Data/Authority Formats
- **Listicle** - "5 reasons why..." or "3 things I wish I knew..."
- **Myth vs Fact** - Challenges common beliefs
- **News/PR Hijack** - Formats content as breaking news or press coverage
- **Expert Interview** - Q&A format with a credentialed professional
- **Ingredient Breakdown** - Deep dive into what is in the product and why

### High-Concept Formats
- **"This is what would happen if..."** - Hypothetical scenario played out
- **Timeline Cascade** - Step-by-step what happens in the body/life
- **Celebrity Reference** - Uses celebrity name or likeness as the hook
- **Analogy / Metaphor** - Unexpected comparison that makes the product point

### Format Selection Notes
The format is NOT the strategy. The format is the vehicle for the strategy. A Credible Narrator delivering an Enemy Framework script is a completely different ad from a Credible Narrator delivering a PAS script, even though both use the same visual format.

Choose the format based on:
1. What creator assets are available
2. What the ad account has NOT tested yet (format diversity matters)
3. What the body structure requires (Timeline Cascade needs visual support, UGC Storytelling needs a relatable creator)
4. What the target platform rewards (TikTok rewards native formats, Meta rewards visual variety)

## PATTERN SUMMARY: WHAT MAKES AN NSY SCRIPT WIN

After analyzing all 27 scripts, these are the non-negotiable patterns:

1. **The hook earns the next 5 seconds, not the sale.** Every winning hook creates a gap - a question, a curiosity, a fear - that can only be resolved by watching more.

2. **Education before selling.** In every T1 script, the product does not appear until at least 40-50% of the runtime. The first half builds trust through empathy, education, or identification.

3. **Specificity over claims.** "18 grams of protein - that's more than two eggs" beats "high protein." "84p per bowl" beats "affordable." "2,000 verified reviews" beats "thousands of happy customers." Every specific number does the work of ten vague adjectives.

4. **The product enters as a friend's recommendation.** No "Introducing..." No "But wait, there's more." The product slides into the conversation like a friend telling you about something that worked for them.

5. **The guarantee is a selling argument.** It is not legal copy at the end. It is positioned as proof that the brand is confident and understands the viewer's skepticism.

6. **Hook variations test different psychological entry points, not different phrasings.** Empathy vs loss framing vs social proof vs statistic. Different doors into the same room.

7. **Voice matches audience.** The creator's accent, pace, energy, and language match the target viewer. The script should sound natural when spoken by the intended creator type.
